## 📖 LATE REPUBLIC STRUCTURE (121-27 BCE)

The Late Republic covers ~94 years of political chaos, civil wars, and the transformation from Republic to Empire. This is Roman history at its most dramatic.

### Key Turning Points

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 121 BCE | Gracchi crisis ends | Violence now acceptable in politics |
| 107 BCE | Marius's consulship | Military reforms - soldiers loyal to generals |
| 91-88 BCE | Social War | Italian allies revolt; citizenship granted |
| 88-82 BCE | Marius vs Sulla | First civil war - armies march on Rome |
| 82-79 BCE | Sulla's dictatorship | Proscriptions, constitutional "reforms" |
| 73-71 BCE | Spartacus revolt | Pompey/Crassus rise to prominence |
| 63 BCE | Catiline conspiracy | Cicero's moment; institutions barely hold |
| 60 BCE | First Triumvirate | Three men secretly control Rome |
| 58-50 BCE | Gallic Wars | Caesar becomes unstoppable |
| 49 BCE | Rubicon crossed | Civil war begins |
| 44 BCE | Ides of March | Caesar assassinated |
| 43-31 BCE | Second Triumvirate | Octavian vs Antony |
| 27 BCE | Augustus | Republic officially dead |

### Topic 1: The System Breaks (121-88 BCE)

**Historical Context**: Post-Gracchi Rome. Violence is normalized, the army transforms, and Italian allies demand citizenship.

**Chapter 1: Marius and the Military Revolution**
- Gaius Marius and the transformation of Roman armies
- The Numidian War and Marius's rise
- Professional soldiers vs. citizen-soldiers (Marian reforms)
- Implications for Roman politics
- The Germanic threat (Cimbri and Teutones)

**Chapter 2: The Social War & Italian Question**
- Who were Rome's Italian allies?
- Why citizenship was denied
- The revolt (91-88 BCE)
- Rome forced to grant citizenship
- Political chaos in the aftermath

---

### Topic 2: Sulla's Republic of Blood (88-79 BCE)

**Historical Context**: Rome's first civil war. Military strongmen clash. The Republic will never be the same.

**Chapter 1: Civil War - Marius vs Sulla**
- Mithridatic War and the eastern command
- Sulla marches on Rome (unprecedented)
- Marius's revenge and the "Marian massacres"
- Sulla returns from the East
- The final confrontation

**Chapter 2: The Dictatorship and Proscriptions**
- Sulla becomes dictator
- The proscription lists - systematic murder
- Constitutional "reforms" (actually a reboot)
- Sulla's retirement and death
- Legacy: violence works in Roman politics

---

### Topic 3: The Unstable Peace (79-60 BCE)

**Historical Context**: Sulla's system fails almost immediately. New strongmen emerge. The Senate is irrelevant.

**Chapter 1: Pompey the Great's Rise**
- Pompey's early career under Sulla
- Lepidus's rebellion
- Sertorius in Spain
- Spartacus and the slave revolt (73-71 BCE)
- Pompey's eastern conquests (Mithridates, pirates)
- Rome's most powerful general

**Chapter 2: Cicero, Catiline, and the Last Republicans**
- Cicero as orator and defender of the Republic
- The Verres trials
- Catiline's conspiracy (63 BCE)
- Cicero's response and the execution debate
- What the conspiracy revealed about Rome's problems

---

### Topic 4: Three Men and a Republic (60-50 BCE)

**Historical Context**: Pompey, Caesar, and Crassus secretly divide Rome between them. The Republic exists in name only.

**Chapter 1: The First Triumvirate**
- Why three rivals formed an alliance
- Caesar's consulship (59 BCE)
- The deal: Pompey gets veterans settled, Caesar gets Gaul
- Crassus's role (wealth as power)
- The Conference of Luca (56 BCE)

**Chapter 2: Caesar in Gaul**
- The conquest of Gaul (58-50 BCE)
- Military genius and political calculation
- Wealth, veterans, and loyalty
- Caesar's popularity in Rome
- Crassus's death at Carrhae (53 BCE)
- The triumvirate fractures

---

### Topic 5: Caesar's War (49-44 BCE)

**Historical Context**: Caesar refuses to give up his command. The Senate refuses to compromise. Civil war.

**Chapter 1: Civil War - Caesar vs Pompey**
- The Senate's ultimatum
- Crossing the Rubicon (49 BCE)
- Pompey flees to Greece
- Pharsalus (48 BCE) - Caesar's decisive victory
- Pompey's death in Egypt
- Caesar in Egypt (Cleopatra)

**Chapter 2: Dictator Perpetuo**
- Mopping up resistance (Africa, Spain)
- Caesar's reforms (calendar, citizenship, colonies)
- Dictator for life
- The growing conspiracy
- Warning signs ignored

**Chapter 3: The Ides of March**
- The conspirators and their motives
- March 15, 44 BCE
- The assassination
- The aftermath - what now?
- Why killing Caesar solved nothing

### Topic 6: Death of the Republic (44-27 BCE)

**Historical Context**: The final convulsions. Another civil war. Octavian emerges victorious and transforms into Augustus.

**Chapter 1: Chaos After Caesar**
- Mark Antony seizes control
- Octavian arrives (Caesar's heir)
- Cicero's Philippics against Antony
- The Liberators' War
- Brutus and Cassius defeated at Philippi

**Chapter 2: The Second Triumvirate**
- Octavian, Antony, Lepidus divide the empire
- The proscriptions return (Cicero dies)
- Antony in the East
- Antony and Cleopatra
- Octavian consolidates the West

**Chapter 3: Actium to Augustus**
- Propaganda war: Roman vs. Eastern queen
- Battle of Actium (31 BCE)
- Deaths of Antony and Cleopatra
- Octavian alone
- The constitutional settlement (27 BCE)
- Augustus: first emperor, end of the Republic
