# Chapter 1: The First March on Rome (88-87 BCE)

## Introduction: The Unthinkable Becomes Possible

In the entire history of the Roman Republic—nearly five centuries by 88 BCE—no Roman general had ever marched his army against the city itself. Enemies had threatened Rome: Hannibal had camped within sight of its walls, and the Gauls had sacked it in ancient memory. But Roman soldiers turning their swords against Roman citizens, a Roman commander leading legions through the sacred boundary of the *pomerium*? It was unthinkable. It violated every tradition, every religious scruple, every understanding of what it meant to be Roman.

Until Lucius Cornelius Sulla did exactly that.

The events of 88 BCE shattered a barrier that could never be rebuilt. Once it was proven that a Roman army would follow its general against Rome itself, the restraint that had held the Republic together for generations was gone. Every ambitious commander who came after—Cinna, Marius, Caesar, Octavian—would know that the legions could be used not just against foreign enemies but against Roman political opponents. The civil wars that would eventually destroy the Republic began on the day Sulla's soldiers crossed the city boundary.

What drove Sulla to this unprecedented act? The immediate cause was a political dispute over a military command. But behind that dispute lay everything that had gone wrong with the Roman Republic over the previous generation: the transformation of the army into professional forces loyal to their generals, the breakdown of political compromise, the normalization of violence in public life, and the desperate ambitions of men who would rather destroy the system than lose within it.

This chapter traces the path to that fateful march—from the distant shores of Asia Minor, where a foreign king had challenged Roman power, to the streets of Rome itself, where Roman soldiers would kill Roman citizens for the first time in the Republic's long history.

## Section 1: Mithridates and the Eastern Crisis

The crisis that would trigger Rome's first civil war began not in Italy but thousands of miles away, in the kingdom of Pontus on the southern shore of the Black Sea. There, an ambitious king saw Rome's distraction during the Social War as his opportunity to build an empire.

### The King of Pontus

Pontus was a kingdom on the southern coast of the Black Sea, in what is now northern Turkey. The region took its name from the Greek Πόντος Εὔξεινος (Pontos Euxeinos) - the "Hospitable Sea," as the Greeks called the Black Sea. It was a land of rugged mountains, fertile river valleys, and coastal cities that had been Greek colonies for centuries.
The kingdom itself was relatively young. It had been founded around 281 BCE by Mithridates I, a Persian nobleman who carved out an independent realm during the chaos following Alexander the Great's death and the wars between his successors. The ruling dynasty claimed descent from the Persian royal house of the Achaemenids - the same bloodline as Darius and Xerxes who had once invaded Greece. This Persian heritage mixed with Greek culture to create a hybrid kingdom: Greek-speaking cities along the coast, Persian traditions at court, and native Anatolian populations in the interior.
For its first century, Pontus was a minor power, overshadowed by the great Hellenistic kingdoms of the Seleucids and Ptolemies. But as those empires weakened and Rome's attention focused westward on Carthage and the Mediterranean, Pontus quietly expanded. By the time Mithridates VI came to power, it controlled much of the Black Sea's southern and eastern shores.

Mithridates VI Eupator was one of the most remarkable figures of the ancient world. King of Pontus from childhood, he had survived assassination attempts by his own mother and emerged as a cunning, ambitious, and ruthless ruler. He was said to speak twenty-two languages, to have developed immunity to poisons by taking small doses throughout his life, and to possess both the cultural sophistication of a Greek and the military prowess of a Persian king.

For decades, Mithridates had watched Roman power expand eastward, absorbing kingdoms and reducing proud monarchs to client status. He understood that Pontus would eventually face the same fate unless he acted first. The Social War, which tied down Rome's legions in Italy from 91 to 88 BCE, gave him his opening.

### The Explosion of 88 BCE

In 88 BCE, Mithridates struck with stunning speed and coordination. His armies swept through the Roman province of Asia (modern western Turkey), overwhelming the minimal Roman forces stationed there. But military conquest was only the beginning.

Mithridates then ordered what became known as the Asian Vespers—a coordinated massacre of Roman and Italian civilians throughout the province. On a single appointed day, the local populations rose up and killed every Roman and Italian they could find: men, women, and children; merchants, tax collectors, and their families. Ancient sources claim 80,000 victims, though the actual number may have been higher or lower. Whatever the precise figure, it was a catastrophe without precedent—the largest mass killing of Roman citizens in history.

The massacre served multiple purposes. It bound the local populations to Mithridates through shared guilt; having participated in killing Romans, they could expect no mercy if Rome reconquered the province. It eliminated the hated Roman tax collectors and moneylenders who had exploited the region for decades. And it sent a message to Rome: the eastern provinces were in open revolt, and half-measures would not suffice to reclaim them.

### Why the Command Mattered

Rome had no choice but to respond with overwhelming force. The eastern provinces were vital—they provided enormous tax revenue, controlled crucial trade routes, and their loss would encourage rebellion elsewhere. More immediately, the massacre of Roman citizens demanded vengeance. Any commander who failed to respond would be politically destroyed; any commander who succeeded would return covered in glory.

The Mithridatic command was therefore the most desirable military appointment available. It promised years of campaigning in wealthy lands, opportunities for plunder on a massive scale, and the kind of military glory that translated directly into political power. The general who defeated Mithridates would return to Rome as its greatest hero since Marius had defeated the Germanic tribes.

Two men wanted that command desperately: the aging Gaius Marius, six-time consul and savior of Rome, who saw it as his path back to relevance; and Lucius Cornelius Sulla, the current consul and Rome's most capable active general, who saw it as rightfully his. Their rivalry would tear the Republic apart.

## Section 2: The Rivals

The conflict between Marius and Sulla was both personal and political, rooted in decades of shared history that had curdled into bitter enmity.

### Gaius Marius: The Fading Hero

By 88 BCE, Gaius Marius was in his late sixties—ancient by Roman standards. His glory days seemed behind him. He had saved Rome from the Germanic tribes, held an unprecedented six consulships, and transformed the Roman army. But his sixth consulship in 100 BCE had ended in disaster when he was forced to turn against his own allies, and he had spent much of the following decade in political eclipse.

The Social War had given Marius a brief return to prominence. He had served competently, though not brilliantly, and had watched younger men like Sulla win the decisive victories. For a man of Marius's towering pride, this was intolerable. He had been Rome's greatest general; he could not accept being superseded.

Marius coveted the Mithridatic command with singular intensity. Victory in the East would restore his military reputation, replenish his wealth through plunder, and demonstrate that he remained Rome's supreme warrior. His advanced age, his deteriorating health, the evident reality that younger commanders were better suited for a grueling eastern campaign—Marius dismissed all such considerations. The command had become, for him, a matter of existential necessity.

### Lucius Cornelius Sulla: The Patient Aristocrat

Sulla was everything Marius was not. Where Marius was a *novus homo* from an Italian country town, Sulla came from the ancient patrician Cornelii—one of Rome's oldest and most distinguished families. Where Marius had clawed his way to prominence through military service and popular politics, Sulla had been born to the expectation of greatness.

Yet Sulla's path to power had been slow. His branch of the Cornelii had fallen on hard times, and Sulla spent his youth in relative poverty—by aristocratic standards—living in a cheap *insula* - Roman apartment building, typically multi-story tenements where the urban poor and lower middle classes lived -  and consorting with actors and entertainers. He was known for his love of pleasure, his drinking, his affairs with both women and men. No one would have predicted greatness for him.

Military service changed everything. Sulla had served under Marius in the Jugurthine War and had been the officer who actually captured Jugurtha—a fact he never let Marius forget, commissioning a signet ring depicting the surrender. He had proved himself brilliant in the Social War, winning crucial victories that established his reputation as Rome's finest general.

Now consul for 88 BCE, Sulla received the Mithridatic command through the proper constitutional process: the Senate assigned provinces to the consuls, and Sulla received the East. It was legally and traditionally his.

### The Feud

The personal animosity between Marius and Sulla ran deep. Sulla had always resented serving under Marius and believed (with some justification) that his role in capturing Jugurtha had been deliberately obscured. Marius, for his part, despised Sulla as a decadent aristocrat who had risen on achievements that rightfully belonged to others.

But the conflict was also political. Marius represented the *populares*—politicians who worked through the popular assemblies and championed causes that appealed to the common people. Sulla aligned with the *optimates*—the conservative senators who believed the Senate should dominate Roman politics. These were not formal parties but loose tendencies, yet they represented fundamentally different visions of how Rome should be governed.

When the Mithridatic command became available, all these tensions converged. Marius wanted the command for personal glory; Sulla believed it was constitutionally his. The populares saw an opportunity to challenge senatorial authority; the optimates saw a threat to proper procedure. Neither side was willing to back down.

## Section 3: The Tribune's Gambit

The man who lit the fuse was a tribune named Publius Sulpicius Rufus. His legislation would make civil war inevitable.

### Sulpicius and His Program

Sulpicius was a fascinating figure—an aristocrat who had allied with the populares and a brilliant orator whom Cicero later praised as perhaps the finest speaker of his generation. His original political goals were relatively moderate: he wanted to distribute the newly enfranchised Italian citizens across all the voting tribes rather than concentrating them in a few tribes where their votes would be marginalized.

This was the unfinished business of the Social War. The citizenship grants of 90-89 BCE had technically made Italians into Romans, but the Senate's decision to register them in only eight of the thirty-five tribes had rendered their citizenship nearly meaningless in practice. Sulpicius proposed to remedy this injustice.

The proposal was popular with the new citizens and their advocates but anathema to the established Roman political class, who saw their voting power threatened. Violence broke out in the Forum. In one confrontation, the son of Sulla's consular colleague was killed.

### The Alliance with Marius

Facing fierce opposition, Sulpicius made a fateful decision: he allied with Marius. In exchange for Marius's support (and, crucially, the support of the veterans and new citizens who revered the old general), Sulpicius added a provision to his legislation that would transfer the Mithridatic command from Sulla to Marius.

The move was legally questionable—the assembly did not traditionally reassign military commands already granted by the Senate—but it was not without precedent. The assembly had assigned Marius the Jugurthine command over the Senate's objections back in 107 BCE. Sulpicius and Marius were following the same playbook.

The legislation passed. By the will of the Roman people, expressed through their assembly, Gaius Marius now held the command against Mithridates. Messengers were dispatched to Sulla's army, which was encamped at Nola in Campania, preparing to sail east, to inform the legions of their new commander.

### Sulla's Choice

Sulla faced a stark choice. He could accept the assembly's decision, surrender his command, and watch Marius sail off to win glory in the East while his own political career collapsed. This was what tradition and law demanded—magistrates were supposed to obey the will of the people, however unjust they believed that will to be.

Or he could fight.

For any previous Roman commander, there would have been no choice. Defying the assembly was unthinkable. But Sulla had something no previous Roman commander had possessed: an army that was personally loyal to him.

This was the bitter fruit of Marius's own reforms. The military changes of two decades earlier had created professional soldiers whose welfare depended on their general, not on the Republic. Sulla's legions had served under him through the Social War. They expected him to lead them to plunder in the East. If Marius took command, there was no guarantee he would retain them—and even if he did, they would be serving under a general who had not earned their loyalty.

When Sulla put the question to his soldiers, they answered with a roar: they would follow him. Against Rome itself, if necessary.

## Section 4: The March on Rome

What followed was without precedent in Roman history. A Roman consul led his Roman legions against the Roman capital.

### Crossing the Boundary

Roman religious law drew a sacred boundary around the city: the *pomerium*. No armed soldiers could cross this line. Generals celebrating triumphs had to leave their armies outside and enter the city as civilians. This was not mere tradition; it was the fundamental distinction between military and civilian space, between the zone of war and the zone of law.

Sulla's officers understood what they were about to do. When he gave the order to march on Rome, all but one of his senior commanders refused to accompany him. They could not bring themselves to violate so fundamental a boundary. The one exception was Sulla's quaestor, Lucius Licinius Lucullus—a man who would later prove his own ambitions in the East.

The common soldiers had no such scruples. Their loyalty was to Sulla, not to traditions they barely understood. When Sulla marched, six legions marched with him—roughly 30,000 men advancing on their own capital.

### The Battle for Rome

Rome kept no standing garrison within or near the city—the pomerium forbade it—and the Republic's legions were scattered across distant provinces. Marius himself held no command; he was a private citizen whose veterans had long since dispersed to their farms. Sulla's legions at Nola were the only significant military force within striking distance of Rome, and he moved before anyone could respond. They attempted to organize resistance with gladiators, armed slaves, and whatever citizens they could muster, but they were hopelessly outmatched. Some sources describe Romans throwing tiles from rooftops at the advancing soldiers—a desperate, futile gesture against professional legions.

The fighting was brief but significant. Roman soldiers killed Roman citizens in the streets of Rome. The sacred boundary had been crossed not just physically but morally. The spell that had protected the Republic for centuries was broken.

Marius fled the city, beginning an extraordinary odyssey of escape and survival that would become legendary. Sulpicius was hunted down and killed, his head displayed on the Rostra in the Forum—the first of many such gruesome displays that would mark the coming decades of civil war. His legislation was declared invalid, passed under duress and contrary to religious law.

### Sulla's Restraint—and Its Limits

Sulla did not immediately unleash the full terror that he would later employ. He executed about a dozen of his opponents—a number that would have seemed shocking before 88 BCE but would seem almost merciful compared to what was coming. He pushed through legislation designed to strengthen the Senate and weaken the tribunes, reversing some of the popular reforms of recent decades.

But he did not establish a permanent military occupation of Rome. He did not declare himself dictator or king. Having made his point—that he could not be stripped of his command—he prepared to depart for the East and the war against Mithridates.

This relative restraint may have reflected Sulla's genuine conservatism: he saw himself as defending the proper constitutional order against radical tribunes and a glory-hungry old man, not as a revolutionary seeking to overthrow the Republic. It may also have reflected practical calculation: he needed to deal with Mithridates, and he couldn't do that while occupying Rome indefinitely.

Whatever his reasoning, Sulla's departure would prove to be a catastrophic miscalculation. He had demonstrated that military force could override the popular will. He had not eliminated the men who would use that lesson against him.


## Section 5: Sulla's Departure and the Power Vacuum

In 87 BCE, Sulla sailed east with his legions to confront Mithridates, leaving behind a Rome that appeared pacified but was in fact seething with resentment.

### The Elections of 87 BCE

Before departing, Sulla oversaw consular elections that he hoped would leave reliable men in charge. One of the new consuls, Gnaeus Octavius, was a solid optimate whom Sulla trusted. The other, Lucius Cornelius Cinna, had sworn an oath to uphold Sulla's arrangements.

Cinna broke that oath almost immediately.

Once Sulla was safely at sea, Cinna revived Sulpicius's program of distributing the new Italian citizens across all the voting tribes. When his fellow consul Octavius resisted, violence erupted once again in the streets of Rome. This time, it was Cinna who was driven from the city—but unlike Marius before him, Cinna had somewhere to go.

### The Return of Marius

Cinna fled to the army still besieging the remnants of the Social War rebels at Nola—the same army Sulla had led before marching on Rome. These soldiers, left behind when Sulla took his legions east, had no particular loyalty to the Senate or to Octavius. They welcomed Cinna and declared for his cause.

But Cinna's most important recruitment was not soldiers but a single man: Gaius Marius.

Marius had spent months as a fugitive, enduring hardships that became the stuff of legend. He had hidden in marshes, been captured and nearly executed, escaped by the sheer force of his personality (according to tradition, the slave sent to kill him fled when Marius demanded "Do you dare kill Gaius Marius?"), and eventually found refuge in Africa among his old veterans. Now, at Cinna's summons, he returned to Italy with a small force of followers.

The old general was transformed by his exile. The Marius who returned was no longer merely ambitious; he was consumed by rage and the desire for vengeance. The men who had driven him from Rome, who had declared him a public enemy, who had forced him to flee through swamps and hide in ruins—they would pay. All of them.

### The Siege of Rome

The combined forces of Cinna and Marius marched on Rome, reversing Sulla's march of the previous year. Once again, the sacred boundary would be crossed. Once again, Roman soldiers would enter the city under arms.

But this time, the outcome would be far bloodier.

Octavius attempted to defend the city but found himself outmatched. The new Italian citizens, denied their promised political rights, flocked to Cinna's banner. Slaves were promised freedom in exchange for fighting. The city was besieged, its food supplies cut off.

After a brief resistance, Rome surrendered. Cinna and Marius entered as conquerors. What followed was the first systematic political massacre in Roman history—a preview of the horrors that would become routine in the decades to come.

## Section 6: The Marian Massacres

The days following Cinna and Marius's entry into Rome in late 87 BCE witnessed atrocities that shocked even an age growing accustomed to political violence.

### Vengeance Unleashed

Marius had compiled a list of enemies in his mind during the long months of his exile. Now he set about destroying them. His soldiers—many of them slaves he had freed and armed—roamed the streets of Rome, killing anyone Marius identified as an opponent. A nod from the old general was a death sentence; a refused handshake meant the victim's head would shortly decorate the Rostra—the great speaker's platform in the Forum, Rome's most sacred civic space, where politicians addressed the people and public business was conducted. The platform that symbolized Roman liberty and debate was transformed into a showcase for political murder.

The consul Octavius refused to flee, insisting that he would die in his consular robes rather than abandon his duty. He was killed sitting in his curule chair, and his head was displayed publicly—the first consul to be murdered in Rome since the city's founding.

Other prominent senators and equestrians were hunted down and killed. Some were murdered in their homes; others were caught fleeing and butchered on the roads outside the city. The property of the dead was confiscated, their families left destitute.

### The Horror

The biographer Plutarch and the historian Appian, both writing in the first and second centuries CE, paint a picture of almost random terror. Marius's slave soldiers, called the Bardyiae, became death squads answerable only to their master. Citizens were killed on suspicion or on grudges; bodies lay unburied in the streets. Plutarch records that Marius himself sank into a kind of murderous depression, killing through momentum as much as calculation, unable or unwilling to stop the slaughter.

The terror lasted for five days before Cinna, more calculating than Marius, had the Bardyiae surrounded and killed—ending the immediate massacres but not erasing their memory. Perhaps dozens or hundreds of prominent Romans died in those days; the sources disagree on numbers, but the psychological impact was immense.

For the first time, Romans understood that political defeat could mean not just loss of office but loss of life—and the lives of family members. The stakes of political competition had become existential. Compromise became harder when failure meant death.

### Marius's Seventh Consulship and Death

Despite the upheaval, the formal mechanisms of the Republic continued to function. Elections were held—though their legitimacy under such conditions was questionable—and Marius secured his seventh consulship for 86 BCE, fulfilling a prophecy that Plutarch records him receiving decades earlier. According to Plutarch's account, however, Marius was by this point in severe physical and psychological decline, plagued by insomnia and excessive drinking.

Seventeen days into his seventh consulship, Gaius Marius died. Plutarch and Appian offer varying explanations: pleurisy, the accumulated toll of decades of campaigning, or mental collapse. Regardless of the immediate cause, the commander who had saved Rome from the Cimbri and Teutones ended his career responsible for the first systematic political massacre in the city's history.

Cinna maintained control of Rome following Marius's death, holding the consulship consecutively from 87 to 84 BCE in violation of the constitutional norm against successive terms. The Republic had effectively become an authoritarian regime sustained by military force and political intimidation, though its institutions continued to operate in form if not in substance.

The central question confronting the Cinnan government was one it could not resolve: Sulla remained in the East with a battle-hardened army, and his return to Italy was inevitable.


## Conclusion: The Point of No Return

The events of 88-87 BCE established patterns that would define Roman politics for the next half-century until the Republic finally collapsed.

First, the taboo against using military force in domestic politics was shattered. Sulla had marched on Rome and suffered no divine punishment, no military defeat, no political destruction. The gods had not intervened; the city had not fallen into ruin. If the unthinkable was also unpunished, then it was no longer unthinkable. Every ambitious commander now knew that the legions could be used to settle political disputes.

Second, political violence had escalated beyond individual murders to systematic massacre. The Marian killings were not a riot or a mob action; they were organized political terror, conducted by armed soldiers against a targeted list of victims. This too established a precedent that Sulla would shortly follow—and surpass—with his infamous proscriptions.

Third, the personal loyalty of soldiers to their generals had proven stronger than any loyalty to the Republic, to law, or to tradition. Sulla's soldiers had followed him against Rome. Marius's veterans had welcomed him back from exile. The army that was supposed to defend the state had become a tool for individual ambition.

Finally, exile was no longer a permanent solution to political problems. Marius had been driven from Rome, declared a public enemy, hunted across the Mediterranean—and he had returned at the head of an army to take terrible revenge. The only way to permanently remove a political enemy was to kill him. This grim logic would drive the proscriptions that awaited.

Sulla, in the East, was winning his war against Mithridates. But everyone understood that the real war—the war for Rome itself—was still to come. Cinna ruled Italy with an army at his back, but that army was smaller than Sulla's, and its soldiers had not spent years hardening themselves against one of Rome's most dangerous enemies.

The first march on Rome had proven a concept. The second would prove it again, with exponentially greater bloodshed. The Republic was not yet dead, but it had learned how to kill itself.

## Key Terms

- ***Pomerium***: The sacred boundary of Rome, within which no armed soldiers could enter
- ***Populares***: Politicians who advanced their careers through the popular assemblies and championed popular causes
- ***Optimates***: Conservative senators who believed in maintaining traditional senatorial authority
- ***Asian Vespers***: The coordinated massacre of Roman and Italian civilians in the province of Asia, ordered by Mithridates in 88 BCE
- ***Bardyiae***: Marius's personal slave-soldiers who carried out the massacres of 87 BCE
- ***Curule chair***: The ceremonial seat of high Roman magistrates, symbolizing their authority
