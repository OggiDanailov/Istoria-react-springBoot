# Chapter 2: Cicero, Catiline, and the Last Republicans (70–62 BCE)

## Introduction

The decade of the 60s BCE was, on the surface, a period of relative stability. The civil wars of the previous generation had ended. Sulla was dead. The Mithridatic threat had been neutralized. Rome's frontiers were secure, its revenues growing, its institutions — bruised and reshaped by decades of crisis — still nominally functioning. A visitor to Rome in 65 BCE might have concluded that the Republic had survived its worst years and was finding its footing again.

That visitor would have been wrong. Beneath the surface, the structural problems that had produced the Gracchan crisis, the Marian revolution, and Sulla's dictatorship remained entirely unresolved. A generation of displaced Italian farmers, veterans of multiple civil wars, ruined aristocrats, and urban poor competed for a shrinking share of a city whose wealth was increasingly concentrated in the hands of a small number of spectacularly rich men. The political class that governed Rome was simultaneously more venal, more factional, and more personally ambitious than at any previous point in Republican history. And the constitutional mechanisms that were supposed to manage these tensions — the Senate, the magistracies, the courts, the popular assemblies — had been so thoroughly discredited by thirty years of crisis that they commanded respect only from those who had no alternative.

Into this environment stepped two men who embodied, between them, the full contradictions of the late Republic. Marcus Tullius Cicero was the Republic's most brilliant defender: a new man from a provincial town who had climbed to the consulship through sheer intellectual force, who believed passionately in the institutions his contemporaries were destroying, and who would spend his career discovering how little those institutions could be made to protect even their most eloquent champion. Lucius Sergius Catilina — Catiline — was, depending on your perspective, either a cynical demagogue who nearly brought Rome down in flames, or a symptom of social desperation so profound that it could no longer be contained within the Republic's increasingly inadequate political structures.

Their confrontation in 63 BCE produced the most fully documented political crisis of the entire Republican period — and one of the most debated. It also produced the moment that would ultimately destroy Cicero, long after he believed he had saved the Republic.

## Section 1: Marcus Tullius Cicero — The New Man

### Origins and the Problem of Status

Marcus Tullius Cicero was born on January 3, 106 BCE, in Arpinum — a small hill town in the Volscian country southeast of Rome, the same town that had produced Gaius Marius a generation earlier. His family was of the equestrian order: prosperous, locally respected, but not senatorial. They had no ancestors who had held Roman magistracies, no family name that commanded automatic deference in the Forum, no inherited network of aristocratic clients stretching across the Roman world.

In the Roman political system, this was a serious disadvantage. The term *novus homo* — "new man" — described someone who was the first in his family to reach the Senate or, especially, the consulship. It was not a neutral description. It carried an implicit accusation: that the man in question had no business aspiring to offices that tradition reserved for families with generations of service behind them. The aristocracy of the late Republic was not, formally, a closed caste — the constitution permitted any citizen to seek any magistracy — but informally, social pressure, the weight of inherited client networks, and the sheer difficulty of competing without family resources made the *novus homo* an exceptional figure rather than a normal one.

Cicero was determined to be exceptional. From childhood, Plutarch records, he showed an intellectual precocity that attracted attention and eventually brought him to Rome for education under the best teachers available. He studied rhetoric under the leading Greek and Latin teachers of his day, law under the great jurists Quintus Mucius Scaevola, and philosophy under visiting Greek philosophers. He traveled to Greece and Asia Minor specifically to continue his rhetorical and philosophical education — a level of intellectual investment unusual even for the aristocratic families who could more easily afford it.

The result was a man of extraordinary intellectual power: the finest Latin prose stylist of antiquity, a philosopher of genuine depth, a legal mind of the first rank, and an orator whose ability to move audiences — to construct arguments, deploy emotion, and frame political questions — was without peer in the Roman world. Quintilian, writing over a century later, would define eloquence itself as the capacity Cicero had demonstrated.

But Cicero was also, as his letters reveal with unusual candor, a man of considerable personal anxiety, political vanity, and sometimes poor judgment. He needed to be seen to be right. He needed recognition. He was capable of magnificent courage and of spectacular political miscalculation, sometimes in the same episode. Understanding his role in the Catilinarian crisis requires holding both sides of this portrait simultaneously.

### The Path to the Consulship

Cicero's political career followed the *cursus honorum* with meticulous correctness — partly from genuine respect for institutions, partly from the political calculation that a *novus homo* could not afford the constitutional irregularities that men like Pompey had gotten away with. He served as quaestor in Sicily in 75 BCE, as aedile in 69 BCE, and as praetor in 66 BCE, winning each office at the earliest legal age. At every step, he built his reputation primarily through the courts rather than through military command — the arena in which he had the clearest advantage over aristocratic competitors.

By the time he stood for the consulship of 63 BCE, he had established himself as Rome's preeminent advocate and had delivered several of the most celebrated speeches in Roman history. His election, which Plutarch describes as achieved with remarkable unanimity, represented the full validation of the path he had chosen: proof that intellectual distinction, forensic brilliance, and a carefully managed public reputation could carry a man from Arpinum to the consulship without a family name or a private army.

## Section 2: The Verres Trials (70 BCE)

### Gaius Verres and the Plunder of Sicily

Before the Catilinarian crisis made Cicero famous throughout the Roman world, it was a prosecution — not a defense — that established his reputation as the Republic's most formidable advocate. In 70 BCE, Cicero prosecuted Gaius Verres, the former governor of Sicily, for the most comprehensively documented case of provincial extortion in Roman history.

Verres had governed Sicily from 73 to 71 BCE with a thoroughness of corruption that even by the standards of late Republican provincial administration was remarkable. He had systematically looted the province's artistic treasures — stripping temples, private homes, and public buildings of paintings, sculptures, and silver plate that represented centuries of accumulated Sicilian and Greek cultural heritage. He had extorted grain from Sicilian farmers far beyond their legal obligations, pocketing the surplus. He had sold judicial decisions to the highest bidder, executing Roman citizens without trial when they could not pay what he demanded. He had, according to Cicero's account, treated the entire island of Sicily as a personal treasury for three years.

The Sicilians came to Cicero because they had nowhere else to turn. The Roman system of provincial governance provided a formal mechanism for prosecuting corrupt governors — the *quaestio de rebus repetundis*, the standing court for extortion cases — but that court was, in 70 BCE, staffed entirely by senators. And senators, as the equestrian order had argued for decades, had a structural interest in protecting their own kind from prosecution. Verres was defended by Quintus Hortensius, the most celebrated advocate in Rome — the same man who would later argue against the *Lex Gabinia* — who expected that a combination of legal skill, aristocratic connections, and jury manipulation would secure an acquittal as it had in so many previous cases.

### Cicero's Tactical Masterstroke

Cicero's approach to the prosecution was an object lesson in legal strategy. Knowing that Hortensius and Verres expected the case to drag through multiple speeches and adjournments until a change in the court's composition or a convenient procedural technicality could secure a dismissal, Cicero did the opposite. In his opening speech — the *Actio Prima in Verrem* — he delivered only a brief introduction, then immediately called witnesses and presented documentary evidence. He had spent fifty days in Sicily gathering testimony and records, producing a quantity of evidence so overwhelming that further delay was impossible.

Verres read the situation clearly. Before Cicero could deliver the extended second speech he had prepared — the *Actio Secunda*, a five-volume masterpiece of prosecutorial rhetoric that survives in full — Verres went into voluntary exile. He was convicted in absentia and fined an enormous sum.

The *Actio Secunda*, published after Verres's exile as a political document rather than a delivered speech, became one of the most widely read texts of the late Republic. Its detailed account of Verres's abuses served simultaneously as a condemnation of one corrupt governor and an indictment of the entire system of senatorial jury composition that had made such abuses possible and profitable. The speech is also an extraordinary work of art history — Cicero's descriptions of the Sicilian masterpieces Verres stole are among the most detailed accounts of Greek art surviving from antiquity.

### What the Verres Case Revealed

The significance of the Verres prosecution extended well beyond the fate of one corrupt governor. It demonstrated several things that would be relevant throughout the subsequent decade.

First, it showed that the courts — even the extortion court — could be made to function if the evidence was overwhelming and the political moment was right. In 70 BCE, the year Pompey and Crassus were dismantling Sulla's constitution, the political establishment was in a poor position to defend one of its most egregious members.

Second, it established Cicero's public identity: not merely as an advocate for hire, but as a champion of provincial subjects against the abuses of the governing class. This identity was politically valuable for a *novus homo* who needed to distinguish himself from the aristocratic establishment he was simultaneously trying to join.

Third, and most importantly for understanding what followed, the Verres case illustrated the fundamental tension at the heart of Republican governance: a system whose institutions were supposed to protect citizens and subjects from arbitrary power, but whose institutions were staffed and controlled by the same class whose abuses they were supposed to check.

## Section 3: The Political Landscape of the 60s BCE

### The Men Around Cicero

By the time Cicero was elected consul for 63 BCE, the political landscape of Rome was dominated by figures whose ambitions and rivalries would shape the next twenty years. Pompey was in the East, prosecuting his campaign against Mithridates under the *Lex Manilia*. Crassus, the wealthiest man in Rome, was in the city, financing political careers and accumulating influence with the methodical patience that was his most distinctive quality. Julius Caesar — at this point in his mid-thirties, having served as quaestor, aedile, and pontifex maximus — was emerging as the most charismatic populist politician of his generation, openly positioning himself as the heir to the Marian tradition.

Caesar's election as *pontifex maximus* in 63 BCE — the head of Rome's state religion — over two much more senior and better-connected candidates was a significant early demonstration of his political reach. He had spent money he did not have to secure the position, reportedly telling his mother on the morning of the election that he would return either as *pontifex maximus* or not at all. He returned as *pontifex maximus*. The episode illustrated both his personal recklessness and his genuine popular support.

Cato — Marcus Porcius Cato, the great-grandnephew of Cato the Censor — had entered public life as the Senate's most rigidly principled defender of what he understood as Republican tradition. He was incorrupt in an age of spectacular corruption, personally brave, intellectually consistent, and politically inflexible to a degree that contemporaries found simultaneously admirable and maddening. Cicero respected him deeply and found him nearly impossible to work with.

These men — Cicero, Caesar, Crassus, Cato, and Pompey in absentia — formed the political context within which the Catilinarian crisis unfolded.

### Social Tensions

The political landscape cannot be understood without the social one. Italy in the 60s BCE was a society under significant stress. The Sullan land confiscations had created a class of dispossessed farmers throughout central Italy — men who had lost their land to Sulla's veterans and had never recovered it. Many had drifted to Rome, adding to an already swollen urban population competing for limited employment and dependent on the grain dole. Others remained in the countryside, nursing grievances that had not diminished with time.

Sulla's veterans themselves, settled on confiscated land in colonies throughout Etruria and elsewhere, had in many cases failed as farmers. Men trained for war and accustomed to military pay did not always make successful smallholders. Many had sold their grants, spent the proceeds, and found themselves in debt — adding a new category of the economically desperate to the existing pool of the dispossessed.

Debt was, by the 60s BCE, a pervasive social crisis. The Roman credit system was informal, personal, and unforgiving: interest rates were high, collateral was typically land, and default meant the loss of everything. The senatorial and equestrian classes were themselves heavily indebted — aristocratic political careers required enormous expenditure on games, public buildings, and electoral bribes — but for the wealthy, debt was a manageable inconvenience. For those without assets to leverage, it was a trap with no exit.

This was the social pool from which Catiline would draw his support, and from which Cicero, looking at the same population, saw the end of civilized order.

## Section 4: Lucius Sergius Catilina

### The Man

Lucius Sergius Catilina came from one of Rome's oldest patrician families — the Sergii claimed descent from one of Troy's defenders, though the family had produced no consul for generations and had declined significantly from whatever eminence it had once possessed. Catiline himself was approximately the same age as Cicero, born around 108 BCE, and had served in the Social War and under Sulla during the civil wars of the 80s BCE. His service under Sulla was not without controversy: he was accused of participating in the proscriptions with particular enthusiasm, reportedly killing his own brother-in-law and using the connection to Sulla's regime to pursue personal vendettas.

Sallust's portrait of Catiline in the *Bellum Catilinae* is one of the most vivid character sketches in Latin literature, and one of the most deliberately constructed. Sallust presents him as a man of extraordinary physical endurance and personal magnetism — capable of remarkable feats of hardship, able to go without sleep, food, or warmth for extended periods — combined with a fundamental moral corruption that had turned his considerable abilities entirely toward destruction. He was, in Sallust's telling, the embodiment of what happened when Roman virtues were divorced from Roman values: courage without honor, energy without purpose, leadership without legitimacy.

Modern historians have treated Sallust's portrait with appropriate skepticism. Sallust was writing with the benefit — or the burden — of hindsight, after the conspiracy had been definitively labeled treasonous and Catiline definitively labeled a villain. The actual Catiline was almost certainly more complex: a genuine political grievance as well as personal ambition, a genuine constituency as well as a band of desperate followers, a genuine program as well as a conspiracy.

### The Political Career

Catiline had served as praetor in 68 BCE and as governor of Africa in 67–66 BCE. His return from Africa was immediately followed by accusations of provincial extortion — ironically, the same charge Cicero had successfully prosecuted against Verres. The charges prevented him from standing for the consulship in 66 BCE, since candidates under legal accusation were barred from election. He was eventually acquitted, but the delay and the political embarrassment had cost him time he could not recover.

He stood for the consulship twice — for the elections of 65 and 64 BCE — and lost both times. His defeats had multiple causes: genuine opposition from the senatorial establishment, the extortion accusations lingering over his reputation, and the simple fact that in a competitive field for a limited number of offices, not every qualified candidate could win. But Catiline interpreted his defeats, not entirely without justification, as a product of the same oligarchic manipulation of electoral processes that excluded men like him from power regardless of their merits.

His program, as it developed through the repeated electoral failures, increasingly centered on a single policy: *tabulae novae* — "new tablets," meaning the cancellation of debts. This was the most explosive political demand of the late Republic. For the hundreds of thousands of Romans and Italians who were drowning in debt with no legal path to relief, debt cancellation was salvation. For the creditor class — which overlapped almost entirely with the senatorial and equestrian orders who ran Rome — it was an existential threat to the entire system of property and credit on which their wealth depended.

## Section 5: The Conspiracy (63 BCE)

### Cicero's Consulship and the Electoral Campaign

When Cicero stood for the consulship of 63 BCE, Catiline was among his competitors. The campaign was vicious even by Roman standards. Cicero's principal rival was not Catiline but Gaius Antonius Hybrida — a man of aristocratic family but limited ability, whose main qualification was his family name and his willingness to make deals. Cicero secured his election partly by striking a private arrangement with Antonius: he exchanged the profitable province of Macedonia (which fell to him by lot) for the less valuable province of Cisalpine Gaul, ensuring Antonius's cooperation throughout the consulship.

Catiline lost the election. He then began organizing what would become the conspiracy.

### The Conspiracy Takes Shape

The conspiracy that Catiline organized through 63 BCE drew together a remarkably diverse collection of the discontented. Its core members included ruined aristocrats — men of good family whose debts had stripped them of their estates and their social standing, for whom debt cancellation was the only path back to the life they felt they deserved. Among these was Publius Cornelius Lentulus Surtus, a former consul who had been expelled from the Senate for misconduct and was seeking rehabilitation; Gaius Cethegus, a violent man whose enthusiasm for the conspiracy's more extreme elements — he reportedly kept a stockpile of weapons in his house — alarmed even his co-conspirators; and a number of younger aristocrats whose careers had stalled or whose debts had become unmanageable.

Beyond Rome, the conspiracy had support in Etruria, where a former Sullan officer named Gaius Manlius was organizing veterans and dispossessed farmers into a military force. Sallust emphasizes the Etrurian dimension: the conspiracy was not merely a coup plot by desperate aristocrats in Rome but had genuine roots in the rural poverty and dispossession that the Sullan settlement had created and left unaddressed.

The plan, in its broadest outlines, involved simultaneous military action in Etruria by Manlius's forces and political action in Rome — including, in the conspiracy's most extreme versions, the assassination of Cicero and key senators, arson in multiple districts of the city, and a general uprising timed to coincide with the moment of maximum confusion.

### Cicero's Intelligence Operation

What Catiline did not know was that Cicero had been receiving intelligence about the conspiracy for weeks before he acted on it. His primary source was Fulvia, the mistress of one of the conspirators — Quintus Curius, a man who had been expelled from the Senate and who had been boasting to Fulvia about the great things that would soon come his way. Fulvia, alarmed, passed the information to Cicero. He also received intelligence from other sources, including — according to some ancient accounts — from Crassus himself, who reportedly delivered to Cicero a packet of letters one night warning him of the conspiracy.

Crassus's role, if real, is one of the great mysteries of the period. He had connections with Catiline and had at various points supported or tolerated his political activities. Whether his delivery of the letters represented genuine alarm at the conspiracy's violence, a calculation that Catiline had become a liability, or a more complex political maneuver is impossible to determine. Cicero, characteristically, used the information while being careful not to implicate Crassus more than necessary — he needed the richest man in Rome as an ally, not an enemy.

### The First Catilinarian — Quo Usque Tandem

On November 8, 63 BCE, Cicero convened the Senate in the Temple of Jupiter Stator on the Palatine Hill — a choice of venue that was itself a statement, since the temple was associated with Rome's deliverance from its enemies. Catiline, with remarkable brazenness, attended. Cicero rose and delivered the first of the four speeches that would become known as the *Catilinarian Orations* — arguably the most famous political speeches in the history of Latin literature.

The opening — *Quo usque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra?* — "How long, Catiline, will you abuse our patience?" — became immediately iconic. It remains one of the most recognized opening lines in all of Latin literature, studied by schoolchildren for two thousand years. The speech was a masterpiece of prosecutorial rhetoric: Cicero laid out what he knew of the conspiracy in sufficient detail to make clear that he knew everything, while being careful not to reveal his sources or commit himself to specific charges he could not yet prove in court. He made it clear to Catiline that Rome knew, that the Senate knew, that he himself knew — and that Catiline's continued presence in the city was an insult to the Republic he had already betrayed.

Plutarch records that the senators seated near Catiline ostentatiously moved away from him as Cicero spoke — a theatrical display of isolation that underscored the speech's effect. Catiline attempted a reply, but was shouted down. That night he left Rome for Etruria to take command of Manlius's forces, a departure that simultaneously confirmed his guilt and removed him from the city.

## Section 6: The Crisis and the Execution Debate

### The Allobroges Affair

With Catiline gone, the conspiracy's Roman leadership — Lentulus, Cethegus, and the others — remained in the city and continued their preparations. Their downfall came through a piece of extraordinary luck combined with Cicero's ruthless exploitation of it.

A delegation of the Allobroges — a Gallic tribe from the region of modern Switzerland and Savoy, who had come to Rome to petition the Senate about their debt burden — was approached by the conspirators and invited to join the plot. The Allobroges were to return to Gaul, raise a cavalry force, and bring it back to Italy to support the uprising. The Allobroges considered the offer, consulted their Roman patron, and — in a decision that must have surprised the conspirators — reported the approach to Cicero.

Cicero persuaded the Allobroges to play along while he prepared to catch the conspirators in the act. On the night of December 2–3, 63 BCE, the Allobroges met with the conspirators on the Milvian Bridge — the same bridge near which Lepidus had been defeated seven years earlier — to receive letters of introduction to Gallic leaders and written guarantees of the promises made to them. Cicero had troops waiting. The conspirators were arrested, the letters seized.

The letters were everything Cicero needed. Addressed in the conspirators' own hands, sealed with their own seals, containing promises of debt cancellation and revolution — they were proof that could not be explained away. Cicero brought the conspirators before the Senate the next day, had the letters read aloud, had the seals identified by the senators themselves, and watched as Lentulus, Cethegus, and the others had no answer to give.

### The Debate: Death or Imprisonment?

What happened next was the most consequential political debate of Cicero's consulship, and the decision he made in it would eventually cost him everything.

The question before the Senate was what to do with the arrested conspirators. Roman law — specifically the *lex Sempronia* passed by Gaius Gracchus — prohibited the execution of Roman citizens without trial. The Senate was not a court; it could not legally sentence anyone to death. The proper procedure was to hold the conspirators for trial before a jury court, at which point they could be prosecuted under the law against treason.

Cicero, however, wanted immediate execution. His reasons were partly practical — imprisoned conspirators might be rescued, trials might drag on while Manlius's army remained in the field — and partly political — a swift, decisive response would demonstrate that the Republic was capable of defending itself and would cement his own reputation as its savior.

The Senate debate began with the consul-elect Decimus Junius Silanus proposing the death penalty — using the careful euphemism "the ultimate punishment" rather than the word "death." Other senators followed Silanus's lead. Then Julius Caesar rose to speak.

### Caesar's Speech

Caesar's intervention in the debate was a masterpiece of forensic and political calculation. He did not argue that the conspirators were innocent, or that their actions did not deserve the severest punishment. He argued, instead, from principle and from precedent: that Roman law categorically prohibited the execution of citizens without trial, that a Senate decree could not override that protection, and that setting the precedent of executing citizens by senatorial decree — however guilty those citizens might be — would create a power that future governments, less scrupulous than the current one, would inevitably abuse.

The argument was constitutionally impeccable. It was also politically devastating for Cicero: if the Senate accepted it, the conspirators would go to trial, the crisis would drag on, and Cicero's moment of triumph would be indefinitely deferred. And Caesar's motives were suspect — he was known to have at least peripheral connections to Catiline's political circle, and some senators believed (without being able to prove) that he had known more about the conspiracy than he had disclosed.

Cicero responded with the fourth Catilinarian oration, urging the Senate to follow Silanus's motion and act decisively. The debate swung back toward execution when Cato rose to speak.

### Cato's Response

Marcus Porcius Cato's speech in favor of execution was, by ancient accounts, the decisive intervention. Where Caesar had argued from law and principle, Cato argued from urgency and Roman tradition: the Senate was not conducting a peacetime trial but facing a wartime emergency, the conspirators had forfeited their rights by making war on the state, and delay was not caution but cowardice.

Plutarch records an interesting aside: during Cato's speech, a note was passed to Caesar from outside the Senate house. Cato, suspicious, demanded that Caesar read it aloud, suggesting it might be from the conspirators. Caesar handed it over. It was a love letter from Cato's half-sister Servilia. Cato, humiliated, threw it back at Caesar with the word "drunkard" — an insult that made him look foolish precisely when he was trying to look formidable. Plutarch tells the story without resolving whether it was coincidence or calculation on Caesar's part.

The Senate voted for execution. Cicero, as consul, was responsible for carrying out the sentence. That same evening — December 5, 63 BCE — he led the five principal conspirators to the *Tullianum*, Rome's ancient underground prison, where they were strangled.

As he left the prison and announced to the crowd that the conspirators were dead, Cicero used a single word — *vixerunt*, "they have lived" — rather than the direct statement that they had been killed. It was a characteristic touch: even in the moment of action, the orator was choosing his words for effect.

## Section 7: Aftermath and the Fall of Cicero

### The End of Catiline

The conspiracy in Rome was broken, but the war in Etruria continued. Gaius Manlius's forces, joined by Catiline after his departure from Rome, remained in the field through the winter of 63–62 BCE. In January of 62 BCE, a consular army under Gaius Antonius — Cicero's former colleague, the man he had struck the deal with during the electoral campaign — engaged Catiline's forces near Pistoria in northern Etruria.

Catiline died in the battle. Sallust's account of his end is one of the most memorable passages in Latin historiography: he died fighting in the front rank, far ahead of where his own lines had been, surrounded by the bodies of enemy soldiers. Whatever else he had been, he died like a Roman soldier. Sallust notes that his face, even in death, retained the fierce expression he had worn in life.

### Cicero's Triumph — and Its Price

Cicero returned to Rome from the anti-Catiline operations as the most celebrated man in the city. The Senate voted him the title *pater patriae* — "father of the fatherland" — the first time in Roman history this honor had been granted to a civilian rather than a military commander. Plutarch records that when Cicero laid down his consulship on December 31, 63 BCE, and attempted to deliver the traditional address to the people summarizing his year in office, the tribune Quintus Caecilius Metellus Nepos — acting on Caesar's instructions — vetoed the speech on the grounds that a man who had executed citizens without trial should not be permitted to address the people. Cicero was allowed only to swear the standard oath that he had upheld the laws.

The veto was a warning of what was coming. Caesar, Crassus, and their allies had not forgotten the December executions, and they had not accepted Cicero's implicit claim that the emergency had justified bypassing the *lex Sempronia*. The legal and political case against Cicero — that he had violated Roman citizens' right to trial by executing the conspirators on a Senate decree alone — was not merely a technical argument. It was a genuine constitutional grievance, and it would be weaponized against him.

In 58 BCE, the tribune Publius Clodius Pulcher — a populist politician with close ties to Caesar — proposed a law making it illegal for any person to give fire and water (the Roman formula for shelter and sustenance) to anyone who had executed Roman citizens without trial. The law was retroactive and transparently aimed at Cicero. Faced with prosecution, Cicero went into exile rather than stand trial — a decision he would spend years regretting and justifying.

He was recalled from exile in 57 BCE through the efforts of Pompey and others, but he returned to a Rome in which the First Triumvirate had already transformed the political landscape beyond recognition. The Republic he had saved in 63 BCE was, by 57 BCE, already dying by other means.

## Section 8: What the Conspiracy Revealed

### The Limits of the Republic's Self-Defense

The Catilinarian conspiracy is sometimes presented as a story of the Republic successfully defending itself against subversion — Cicero the hero, Catiline the villain, the institutions holding. This reading is not entirely wrong. The conspiracy was genuinely dangerous, the response was effective, and Rome did not burn in 63 BCE.

But a more honest assessment reveals a more troubling picture. The conspiracy succeeded in being detected and suppressed not because Rome's institutions functioned normally, but because Cicero received private intelligence from a mistress and a delegation of foreign debtors chose to inform rather than rebel. The Senate's response, when it came, immediately ran into the constitutional problem that the law prohibited exactly what the emergency appeared to require. The debate between Caesar and Cato — both of whom were making arguments that were partly principled and partly self-interested — illustrated that even Rome's most serious political thinkers could not agree on whether the Republic's rules applied to its own survival.

Cicero's decision to execute the conspirators without trial was courageous in the immediate sense and politically disastrous in the long-term sense. It saved Rome in December 63 BCE and ensured Cicero's exile in 58 BCE. More significantly, it established a precedent that the Senate could authorize the killing of citizens in emergencies — a precedent that, like Sulla's proscriptions, could be invoked again under less scrupulous auspices.

### The Social Problem Untreated

The conspiracy also revealed, with brutal clarity, that the social crisis underlying it — debt, dispossession, the economic desperation of the Italian countryside — had not been addressed by any of the constitutional reforms of the preceding decades. Sulla's reorganization had done nothing for the dispossessed. Pompey's eastern wealth had gone to Pompey and his veterans. The grain dole sustained the urban poor at subsistence level but offered no path to recovery for the rural dispossessed.

Sallust, whose analysis of the conspiracy remains the most penetrating written within living memory of the events, argued that Catiline was not the cause of Rome's crisis but its symptom. The underlying conditions — the concentration of wealth, the destruction of the small farmer class, the corruption of the political system, the willingness of the governing class to use any means necessary to preserve its position — would outlast Catiline and would eventually produce a crisis that could not be suppressed by a brilliant speech and a timely arrest.

### Caesar's Lesson

Julius Caesar's role in the conspiracy debate deserves particular attention. He had almost certainly known more about Catiline's plans than he disclosed. His speech against the executions was constitutionally correct and politically calculated. His use of a tribunician veto to silence Cicero's farewell address was a precise demonstration of how the restored tribunate could be weaponized against a consul who had overreached.

What Caesar took from 63 BCE was a set of lessons he would apply over the following two decades. He saw that social desperation — debt, dispossession, the grievances of the rural poor — could generate political force that the Senate's normal mechanisms could not contain. He saw that the constitutional rules could be argued both ways, and that a sufficiently clever advocate could make the legal case for almost any position. And he saw that Cicero — brilliant, principled, ultimately limited by his own respect for institutions — was a man who could be outmaneuvered by someone willing to play by different rules.

When Caesar crossed the Rubicon in 49 BCE, the road that led him there ran partly through the winter of 63 BCE, and through the unresolved questions that Cicero's consulship had raised and failed to answer.

## Key Terms

- ***Novus homo***: "New man" — a Roman citizen who was the first in his family to reach the Senate or the consulship. Cicero was the most celebrated *novus homo* of the late Republic, achieving the consulship without family connections or military command through forensic brilliance and carefully managed public reputation.
- ***Quaestio de rebus repetundis***: The permanent standing court for cases of provincial extortion — the court before which Gaius Verres was prosecuted in 70 BCE. Under Sulla's reforms, juries in this court were entirely senatorial; under the reform of 70 BCE, they were mixed.
- ***Tabulae novae***: "New tablets" — the cancellation of debt records, the central political demand of Catiline's program. The phrase was a euphemism drawn from the physical tablets on which debt agreements were recorded; "new tablets" meant starting fresh. It was the most explosive economic demand of the late Republic.
- ***Lex Sempronia***: The law passed by Gaius Gracchus prohibiting the execution of Roman citizens without trial. It was the law Cicero violated — or circumvented — when he ordered the execution of the Catilinarian conspirators on the basis of a Senate decree, and the legal basis on which he was later prosecuted and sent into exile.
- ***Pater patriae***: "Father of the fatherland" — the honorific title voted to Cicero by the Senate for his role in suppressing the Catilinarian conspiracy. It was the first time the title had been granted to a civilian. Cicero would refer to it with pride for the rest of his life; his enemies would use it as evidence of his insufferable vanity.
- ***Senatus consultum ultimum***: The "ultimate decree of the Senate" — an emergency resolution instructing the consuls to take whatever measures were necessary to defend the Republic. The legal validity of this decree as authorization for extraordinary measures (including the execution of citizens) was the central constitutional question of the conspiracy debate.
- ***Tullianum***: Rome's ancient underground prison, located at the foot of the Capitoline Hill. Political prisoners condemned to death were strangled there by the public executioner. The Catilinarian conspirators were executed in the Tullianum on December 5, 63 BCE.
- ***Vixerunt***: "They have lived" — the word Cicero used to announce the execution of the conspirators to the crowd outside the Tullianum, avoiding the direct statement that he had ordered Roman citizens killed. The choice of the perfect tense — "they lived" rather than "they were killed" — was a characteristic piece of rhetorical precision.
- ***Actio Prima / Actio Secunda***: The two phases of a Roman prosecution. The *Actio Prima* was Cicero's opening speech at the Verres trial; the *Actio Secunda* was the extended five-volume prosecution speech he had prepared but never delivered, published afterward as a literary and political document.
- ***Pontifex Maximus***: The head of Rome's state religion, responsible for overseeing religious ceremonies, the calendar, and the priestly colleges. Caesar's election to this position in 63 BCE — over much more senior candidates — was an early demonstration of his popular political reach.

## Timeline

- **106 BCE**: Cicero born at Arpinum
- **91–88 BCE**: Social War; Catiline serves in the Roman army
- **80s BCE**: Catiline serves under Sulla during the civil wars; accused of participating in proscriptions
- **75 BCE**: Cicero serves as quaestor in Sicily — his first encounter with the province he will later defend
- **73–71 BCE**: Verres governs Sicily with systematic corruption
- **70 BCE**: Cicero prosecutes Verres; Verres goes into voluntary exile before sentencing
- **68 BCE**: Catiline serves as praetor
- **67–66 BCE**: Catiline governs Africa; returns to face extortion charges that delay his consular candidacy
- **66 BCE**: Catiline's first failed consular candidacy
- **65 BCE**: Catiline's second failed consular candidacy
- **63 BCE**: Cicero elected consul; Caesar elected pontifex maximus
- **63 BCE**: Catiline's third consular candidacy fails; conspiracy begins to take shape
- **8 November 63 BCE**: Cicero delivers the First Catilinarian oration in the Temple of Jupiter Stator; Catiline leaves Rome for Etruria
- **2–3 December 63 BCE**: Allobroges delegation arrested on the Milvian Bridge; conspirators' letters seized
- **3 December 63 BCE**: Conspirators brought before the Senate; letters read and authenticated
- **5 December 63 BCE**: Senate debate — Caesar argues against execution, Cato argues for; Senate votes for death
- **5 December 63 BCE**: Cicero executes five conspirators in the Tullianum; announces their deaths with *vixerunt*
- **January 62 BCE**: Battle of Pistoria; Catiline killed fighting in the front rank
- **62 BCE**: Pompey returns from the East; First Triumvirate begins to form
- **58 BCE**: Tribune Clodius passes law targeting Cicero for executing citizens without trial; Cicero goes into exile
- **57 BCE**: Cicero recalled from exile through Pompey's intervention

## Ancient Sources for This Chapter

- **Cicero** (the *Catilinarian Orations*, *Pro Murena*, *In Verrem*, letters and speeches): Cicero is simultaneously the primary actor and the primary source for most of this chapter — an unusual historiographical situation that requires constant awareness of his perspective. The four *Catilinarian Orations* are essential reading; the *In Verrem* speeches (particularly the *Actio Secunda*) are the primary source for the Verres prosecution. His letters — particularly those collected in the *Epistulae ad Atticum* — provide invaluable personal testimony about the political atmosphere of the 60s BCE.
- **Sallust** (*Bellum Catilinae*): Written approximately twenty years after the events, the *Bellum Catilinae* is the most systematic ancient account of the conspiracy and the closest in time of any extended treatment. Sallust was a contemporary of many participants and had access to sources now lost. His analysis of the social causes of the conspiracy is the most penetrating in antiquity. His bias against the senatorial establishment and his admiration for Caesar require consideration when evaluating his account.
- **Plutarch** (*Life of Cicero*, *Life of Cato the Younger*, *Life of Caesar*, *Life of Crassus*): Plutarch's *Life of Cicero* is the most detailed surviving biography and provides rich anecdotal material about the conspiracy debate, the executions, and Cicero's subsequent exile. His portraits of Cato and Caesar in the Senate debate are essential.
- **Cassius Dio** (*Roman History*, Books 37–38): Provides a detailed narrative of the conspiracy and the Senate debate, with particular attention to the constitutional questions raised by the executions. Writing under the Severan emperors, Dio had a sophisticated understanding of the Republican constitution's contradictions.
- **Appian** (*Civil Wars*, Book 2): Covers the conspiracy as part of his broader narrative of the late Republic's collapse, providing useful context for understanding its place in the longer sequence of crises.
- **Asconius** (*Commentary on Cicero's speeches*): A first-century CE scholar whose commentary on several of Cicero's speeches preserves historical information about the political context of the trials and orations that is not available elsewhere.
- **Quintilian** (*Institutio Oratoria*): Essential for understanding Cicero's place in Roman rhetorical tradition and the reception of his oratorical achievements by later generations.
