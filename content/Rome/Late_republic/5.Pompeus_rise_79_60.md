# Chapter 1: Pompey the Great — From Sulla's Lieutenant to Rome's Master (79–62 BCE)

## Introduction

When Sulla retired to his Campanian estate in 79 BCE, he left behind a Rome that had been remade — on paper, at least. The constitution had been rewritten, the Senate packed with his supporters, the tribunate castrated, and the courts reorganized. What he did not leave behind was a successor capable of defending his settlement, or any mechanism for doing so that did not rely on the same military force he had used to impose it.

The decade that followed his death in 78 BCE was defined by a series of overlapping crises, none of which the Sullan constitution proved capable of managing: a military revolt in Italy, a rogue Roman general commanding a rebel state in Spain, and the largest slave uprising in Roman history. Each crisis was resolved not by the Senate acting within constitutional norms, but by individual commanders wielding extraordinary powers. And the most consequential of those commanders was a young man who had attached himself to Sulla before he held any legitimate magistracy, and who would spend the rest of his career seeking the kind of supremacy his mentor had demonstrated was possible.

Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus — Pompey the Great — was the dominant figure of the generation between Sulla and Caesar. His career spanned three continents, six major commands, and nearly thirty years of Roman public life. He fought pirates in the Mediterranean, kings in the East, and rebels across the breadth of the Roman world. He reorganized half the known world, generated wealth that dwarfed anything Rome had previously accumulated from conquest, and returned to Italy in 62 BCE as the most powerful private individual in Roman history.

He then failed, comprehensively, to convert that power into lasting political dominance — and his failure created the conditions for Julius Caesar's rise. Understanding how Pompey rose, what methods he used, and why his dominance ultimately collapsed is essential to understanding how the Republic died.

## Section 1: Sulla's Young Butcher — An Unconstitutional Beginning

### The Man From Picenum

Gnaeus Pompeius was born in 106 BCE into a wealthy family from Picenum in northeastern Italy. His father, Gnaeus Pompeius Strabo, had been a consul and a capable if ruthless military commander during the Social War — a man respected for his competence and feared for his cruelty, who had managed to profit from both sides of Rome's political conflicts before dying of plague in 87 BCE. Young Pompey inherited his father's client networks in Picenum, his veterans, and his talent for military organization, along with a substantial private fortune.

When Sulla landed at Brundisium in 83 BCE, Pompey was twenty-three years old and held no public office. He had never been elected to any magistracy, commanded no troops by any legal authority, and possessed no constitutional standing whatsoever. None of this deterred him. Drawing on his family's connections in Picenum, he raised three legions from his father's former clients and veterans, marched south, and presented himself and his army to Sulla as a ready-made ally.

Sulla's response set the tone for everything that followed. The dictator dismounted from his horse, greeted the young man as *imperator* — the honorific title awarded to a victorious commander by his troops — and showed him greater honor than he showed men of far greater seniority and legal standing. Plutarch notes that even Sulla's most senior officers were astonished. The episode was Pompey's introduction to Roman public life: he learned immediately that military force and personal loyalty to the right patron could substitute entirely for the constitutional prerequisites that the *cursus honorum* was supposed to require.

### Sicily, Africa, and the First Triumph

Sulla put Pompey's army to immediate use. In 82 BCE, with the Italian campaign concluded, Sulla dispatched him to Sicily to deal with the Marian holdout Gnaeus Papirius Carbo. Pompey crossed to the island, rapidly crushed resistance, captured Carbo, and had him executed — earning the nickname *adulescentulus carnifex*, "the young butcher," from his critics. From Sicily, Sulla sent him to Africa, where Marian forces under Gnaeus Domitius Ahenobarbus had gathered with the support of the Numidian king Hiarbas. The campaign took forty days. Pompey destroyed the Marian army, killed Domitius in battle, and restored Rome's allied king to power.

When he returned to Italy, Pompey made a remarkable demand: he requested a triumph despite being, technically, a private citizen with no magistracy, no *imperium* granted by the Senate, and no legal standing to receive such an honor. By any reading of Roman law, the request was absurd. Sulla initially resisted, pointing out the legal impossibility. Pompey reportedly responded that more people worshipped the rising sun than the setting sun — a pointed reminder of where power actually lay. Sulla granted the triumph. Pompey had established the central principle of his career: that military success, broadly advertised and carefully stage-managed, could override any constitutional obstacle.

## Section 2: The Revolt of Lepidus (78–77 BCE)

### An Unexpected Challenger

Marcus Aemilius Lepidus was one of the more improbable figures of the late Republic: a man who had made a fortune from the proscriptions — purchasing confiscated estates at depressed prices, much as Crassus had done — and who then turned against the Sullan system that had enriched him. In 78 BCE, the year of Sulla's death, he was elected consul. From the moment he took office, Lepidus agitated against Sulla's constitutional arrangements: he proposed the recall of exiles, the restoration of confiscated properties, and the revival of grain distributions that Sulla had curtailed.

The Senate extracted an oath from both consuls not to take their armies against each other. Lepidus swore it and almost immediately violated it. After his consulship ended, he went to his province of Cisalpine Gaul, raised troops among the dispossessed farmers whose lands had been given to Sulla's veterans, and marched his army toward Rome — demanding a second consulship in direct violation of Sulla's ten-year interval law.

### Pompey Saves the Sullan Settlement

The Senate turned to Pompey, who again held no qualifying magistracy. They granted him and his colleague Catulus military commands to deal with Lepidus — a decision that required pretending that Pompey had some legal basis for commanding an army. He did not. The Senate simply chose not to notice. The military outcome was swift: Lepidus was defeated north of Rome, driven from Italy, and died in Sardinia shortly afterward.

The episode established patterns that would recur throughout the next generation. The Senate, when faced with a military crisis, set aside its own constitutional rules to empower a man it found useful. The underlying problem — the rage of Italians dispossessed by Sullan land grants — was not addressed at all. And Pompey had now demonstrated, for the second time, that emergency commands could be granted outside normal constitutional channels whenever the Senate was frightened enough.

## Section 3: The Sertorian War (80–72 BCE)

### An Alternative Rome in Spain

Of all the extraordinary figures thrown up by the late Republic's decades of civil conflict, Quintus Sertorius is among the most remarkable. A Marian commander of genuine talent — he had lost an eye during the Social War — Sertorius had fled to Spain when Sulla returned to Italy rather than face either defeat or capitulation. What he built there over nearly a decade was something unprecedented: an alternative Roman state, governed according to Roman institutions, in which he presented himself not as a rebel but as the legitimate representative of the Republic against Sulla's illegal regime.

Sertorius was not merely a military commander in a foreign province. He established a Senate-in-exile of Roman refugees, founded a school at Osca to educate the children of Spanish nobles in Latin and Roman customs, distributed Roman citizenship to his Spanish allies, and created a genuinely multi-ethnic army that combined Roman tactical discipline with the guerrilla expertise of the Iberian landscape. He maintained diplomatic contacts with Mithridates of Pontus, who was already planning his next war against Rome. Plutarch's *Life of Sertorius* presents him as one of the most sympathetic figures of the period: a man who in different circumstances might have been among Rome's greatest servants, but whom the chaos of civil war had turned into Rome's most dangerous enemy.

The white fawn that he kept as a companion and presented to his Spanish allies as a gift of Diana — claiming it provided him with divine guidance — became one of the most famous propaganda devices of the age.

### Pompey Dispatched

Rome sent a succession of commanders to deal with Sertorius, and a succession failed. Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius found himself consistently outmaneuvered; Sertorius avoided pitched battles when they were disadvantageous, retreated into difficult Iberian terrain, and wore down opponents through exhaustion rather than direct confrontation. By 77 BCE the situation was serious enough that additional forces were needed.

Once again, Pompey was given a military command despite holding no qualifying magistracy. He was not even a senator — he had never held the quaestorship required for Senate membership. The legal contortions required to justify his command were elaborate, and Pompey performed them impatiently. Plutarch records that when the decree granting his command was being read aloud, Pompey publicly threatened to stay home unless the Senate provided adequate supplies — a display of leverage that left his nominal superiors in no doubt about who actually held power.

### Ended by Murder, Not Battle

Pompey's Spanish campaign (77–72 BCE) was a prolonged and frustrating experience that did not especially enhance his military reputation. Against Sertorius in the field, he suffered several notable reverses. At the Battle of Lauron (76 BCE), Sertorius outmaneuvered him completely, destroying a Roman force while Pompey watched helplessly. At Sucro (75 BCE), Pompey was personally wounded and nearly killed. Metellus, the senior commander, had to rescue him on at least one occasion.

What ended the Sertorian War was not military genius but treachery. In 72 BCE, Sertorius was assassinated at a dinner party by his own lieutenant, Marcus Perperna Vento, who had grown jealous of his authority. Perperna promptly demonstrated why Sertorius had been indispensable: he engaged Pompey in open battle, was decisively defeated, captured, and executed. Pompey characteristically burned Perperna's papers without reading them — they reportedly contained correspondence implicating numerous prominent Romans in dealings with Sertorius, and Pompey had no interest in creating more enemies than necessary.

## Section 4: Spartacus and the Slave War (73–71 BCE)

### The Uprising

While Pompey was fighting in Spain, Italy itself erupted in the most dangerous slave revolt in Roman history. In 73 BCE, a group of gladiators broke out of a training facility at Capua in Campania. Their leader was a Thracian slave named Spartacus — described by Plutarch as a man of exceptional intelligence and culture, unusual for his circumstances. What began as a breakout by perhaps seventy men rapidly became something far more alarming. Spartacus and his followers seized weapons, fortified themselves on Mount Vesuvius, and began attracting runaway slaves from the surrounding countryside. Within months the force had grown to tens of thousands.

Rome sent a praetor with two legions to suppress them; Spartacus routed both. A second praetorian force was destroyed. By 72 BCE, Spartacus commanded an army estimated by ancient sources at anywhere between 70,000 and 120,000. The slave army moved north through Italy, winning battles against every Roman force sent against it. At one point Spartacus apparently planned to lead his followers across the Alps and disperse them back to their homelands — a relatively modest aim that his allied commanders, who preferred to continue raiding Italy's wealthy estates, repeatedly frustrated. The strategic direction of the revolt was thus never coherent: some factions wanted to escape, others to ravage Italy, and Spartacus was never able to impose a unified purpose.

### Crassus Takes Command

After the repeated failure of praetorian-level commanders, the Senate appointed Marcus Licinius Crassus — the wealthiest man in Rome, whose fortune had been built partly on the proscriptions — to command with eight legions. Crassus imposed brutal discipline on troops who had been performing poorly, reportedly reviving the ancient punishment of *decimation*: the random execution of one soldier in ten from units that had fled in battle.

Crassus cornered Spartacus in the toe of Italy and constructed an extraordinary fortification — a wall and ditch running across the entire width of the peninsula, roughly fifty kilometers, to trap the slave army and cut off its forage. Spartacus broke through on a winter night with a portion of his forces, but the breakout was costly and the slave army began to fragment. The final battle came in 71 BCE in Lucania. Spartacus was killed in the fighting — his body was never identified among the dead, though those who saw him fall confirmed he was surrounded by enemy soldiers when he went down.

The aftermath was deliberately horrific. Crassus crucified approximately six thousand captured slaves along the Appian Way from Capua to Rome — a display of calculated terror intended to demonstrate the consequences of rebellion. The bodies were left to rot as a warning.

### Pompey's Irritating Intervention

At this point, a familiar figure reappeared. Pompey, returning from Spain with his victorious army, encountered the remnants of Spartacus's forces fleeing northward and destroyed them — killing approximately five thousand. He then sent a dispatch to the Senate claiming credit for finishing the war. Crassus, who had done virtually all the actual fighting over two years, was furious. Pompey's dispatch was deliberately calculated: by arriving at precisely the right moment and eliminating the final fugitives, he claimed he had "pulled out the roots" of the rebellion while Crassus had merely won the battles. The Senate awarded Crassus only an *ovatio* — a lesser form of triumph — while acknowledging Pompey's contribution to the war's conclusion.

The incident crystallized the rivalry between the two men that would shape Roman politics for the next twenty years. Plutarch captures the contrast: Crassus rich, methodical, jealous of credit, and deeply resentful of Pompey's talent for claiming glory he had not entirely earned; Pompey glamorous, politically instinctive, and utterly without shame about the former.

## Section 5: The Consulship of 70 BCE — Dismantling Sulla's Constitution

### Two Outsiders Take Office

In 70 BCE, Pompey and Crassus stood jointly for the consulship. Neither was remotely qualified under Sulla's constitution. Pompey had never held any magistracy below the consulship — not the quaestorship, not the aedileship, not the praetorship — and was technically too young for the office. He was not even technically a senator. Crassus had held the praetorship but had skipped the required interval between offices. Both candidacies were, by the letter of the Sullan constitution, illegal.

They were elected anyway. The Senate and the Roman people were not in a position to refuse two men who between them commanded most of Rome's available military force. Pompey reportedly had to ask a friend what the duties of the quaestorship were — the entry-level office he had never held — before taking an office that was supposed to require having held every lower office first.

### The Sullan Settlement Undone

The consulship of Pompey and Crassus was the moment Sulla's constitutional settlement effectively died. The two consuls immediately dismantled its central provisions.

The restrictions Sulla had placed on the tribunate were repealed in full. Tribunes were once again permitted to propose legislation directly to the popular assemblies without senatorial approval. The permanent bar on tribunes seeking further office was removed. The popular reaction to the restoration was reportedly ecstatic.

The composition of juries in the standing criminal courts was changed again. Rather than restoring the purely equestrian juries of the Gracchan period or retaining Sulla's purely senatorial juries, a compromise was implemented: juries would be drawn equally from senators, equestrians, and a new category called *tribuni aerarii* — a wealthy class just below equestrian rank. This diluted senatorial control over the courts significantly.

Censors were elected for the first time since Sulla's dictatorship. They promptly expelled sixty-four senators from the body — men enrolled by Sulla who fell below the required standards of conduct or property.

The Sullan settlement had lasted barely nine years. But what replaced it was not a healthy Republic — it was a power vacuum in which the Senate could no longer reliably control the men it nominally commanded. The real lesson of the 70s BCE was that constitutional forms mattered less than legions, and that the tribunate, however restored, was less important than the armies waiting in the provinces.

## Section 6: The Pirate War and the *Lex Gabinia* (67 BCE)

### The Mediterranean Under Siege

Piracy in the ancient Mediterranean was not a marginal phenomenon. By the 70s BCE it had reached a scale that Romans described as a crisis without precedent. The pirates of Cilicia, organized under semi-permanent leadership structures that Plutarch describes as resembling a military confederation, operated with near-impunity across the entire sea. They raided coastal cities, kidnapped Roman citizens for ransom — including, famously, a young Julius Caesar, seized while sailing to Rhodes and held for thirty-eight days — and disrupted the grain shipments from Sicily, Sardinia, and Africa on which Rome's urban population depended.

The disruption to grain supply was the immediate political crisis. Grain prices in Rome had risen sharply, and the urban poor were restless. Several commanders had been given commissions against the pirates over the preceding decades and achieved only limited local results. Marcus Antonius had received a wide anti-piracy command in 74 BCE, managed to be defeated by the Cretan pirates, and died in 71 BCE before he could recover from the embarrassment.

### An Unprecedented Command

In 67 BCE, a tribune named Aulus Gabinius proposed a law that contemporaries immediately recognized as revolutionary. The *Lex Gabinia* would grant a single unnamed commander — clearly intended to be Pompey — an *imperium* over the entire Mediterranean Sea and all coastline within fifty miles of the sea, for three years, superior to that of all other Roman officials within its territory. The commander could appoint up to twenty-four subordinate legates with praetorian rank, draw on the treasury for unlimited funding, and raise as many troops and ships as he deemed necessary.

The Senate reacted with genuine alarm. The command as described was, in scope, closer to a monarchy than to any previous Roman magistracy. Quintus Hortensius argued that concentrating such power in one person was precisely what the Republican constitution was designed to prevent. Catulus asked what the people would do if Pompey died — pointing out that they had made themselves dependent on a single individual. These objections were drowned out. The popular meeting turned violent when opponents attempted to speak. The *Lex Gabinia* passed with overwhelming popular support.

### Three Months

What followed was one of the most impressive demonstrations of military organization in Roman history. Pompey divided the Mediterranean into thirteen sectors, assigned a subordinate commander to each, and coordinated a simultaneous sweep that pushed the pirates from west to east, driving them toward Cilicia where their main bases were concentrated.

Within forty days the western Mediterranean was clear. Within three months the entire sea had been swept, the Cilician bases captured or destroyed, and the pirate fleets either sunk or surrendered. Rather than executing the tens of thousands who surrendered, Pompey resettled them inland — reasoning that men who had become pirates through poverty could be made into productive subjects if given land and a settled life. His critics later cited this policy as dangerous clemency; his admirers called it enlightened governance. Either way, it worked: Mediterranean piracy did not return as a systemic problem for a generation.

The speed of the campaign was also political. By finishing in three months rather than three years, Pompey had made his point with maximum efficiency — and was now positioned to request an even larger command.

## Section 7: The Third Mithridatic War and the *Lex Manilia* (66–62 BCE)

### Lucullus and the Eastern War

Mithridates VI of Pontus had not been idle since Sulla's relatively lenient peace of 85 BCE. He had spent the intervening years rebuilding his armies, training them in Roman tactical methods, and waiting for the opportunity that Rome's internal conflicts would eventually provide. That opportunity came in 74 BCE when Rome's ally King Nicomedes of Bithynia died and bequeathed his kingdom to Rome. Mithridates invaded, beginning the Third Mithridatic War.

The consul Lucius Licinius Lucullus took command and demonstrated genuine strategic talent. He refused to fight Mithridates's main army in pitched battle, instead cutting its supply lines and forcing repeated retreats through starvation. By 72 BCE he had driven Mithridates out of Pontus and was pursuing him into Armenia, where the king had taken refuge with his son-in-law, the Armenian king Tigranes the Great.

Lucullus then made the bold decision to pursue the war into Armenia itself — far beyond his original mandate. He defeated Tigranes's enormous army at Tigranocerta (69 BCE) and again at Artaxata (68 BCE), victories Plutarch describes as among the greatest military achievements of the age. But extended campaigns exhausted his soldiers, who had been in the field for years far from Italy. Lucullus was also a man of severe personal standards — demanding, unsympathetic to complaints, genuinely uncorrupt — which made him respected but not loved. Mutiny followed. His soldiers refused to advance further, and Mithridates returned to Pontus behind Lucullus's distracted back, beginning to reconquer lost territory.

Lucullus had also reorganized the finances of Asia Minor in ways that protected the local population but severely damaged the interests of the Roman equestrian tax-farmers (*publicani*) who collected revenues there. The equestrian order became active enemies of his command. When the question of a successor came before the Roman assemblies, the answer was inevitable.

### The *Lex Manilia*

In 66 BCE, the tribune Gaius Manilius proposed a law transferring command of the eastern war to Pompey — adding authority over all Roman provinces in the East and all forces stationed in them, including Lucullus's veteran legions, to his already extraordinary powers from the *Lex Gabinia*.

The political debate over the *Lex Manilia* was the occasion for one of the most important surviving speeches of Cicero's early career. Cicero, serving as praetor that year, supported the bill enthusiastically and used the speech to articulate the qualities required of a great commander: military knowledge, courage, authority, and good fortune — all of which, he argued, Pompey possessed in unequaled measure. The speech is remarkable not only for its eloquence but for what it reveals about the Republic's transformation. Cicero was, by temperament and conviction, a defender of senatorial authority. Yet here he was arguing that one man should control virtually the entire Roman East because no institutional alternative could prosecute the war effectively. The implication — that the Senate was incapable of managing Rome's affairs through normal means — damaged the very institutions he professed to defend.

The *Lex Manilia* passed easily. Lucullus, recalled and stripped of his command, met Pompey for a bitter transfer-of-command interview. He told Pompey he was arriving like a bird of prey to take credit for another animal's kill. Pompey replied he had been assigned to finish a difficult job and intended to do so. Both men were partly right.

### Mithridates Defeated

Pompey pursued Mithridates northward along the eastern shore of the Black Sea and defeated his army decisively in a night engagement near the River Lycus in Pontus (66 BCE). Mithridates fled westward along the Black Sea coast into the Crimea. He never returned to Pontus. In his final years he attempted to raise a new army among the Scythian peoples, dreaming of a march through the Balkans to Italy itself. His plans were overtaken by a revolt led by his own son Pharnaces. Betrayed and abandoned, Mithridates attempted suicide by poison — but he had spent so many years taking antidotes against assassination that the poison failed. According to Appian, he finally had a Gallic soldier run him through with a sword. He died in 63 BCE, ending a conflict that had dominated Roman foreign policy for over twenty years.

### Reorganizing the East

What Pompey undertook over the following years (65–62 BCE) was a comprehensive reorganization of Rome's eastern possessions that transformed the region's political geography and generated wealth on a scale Rome had rarely seen from conquest.

Pontus was converted into a Roman province, combined with Bithynia. Syria, fragmented into competing petty dynasties after the collapse of Seleucid power, was organized as a new Roman province. Pompey captured Damascus and moved south through Palestine, where a civil war between rival Hasmonean claimants to the Jewish kingship had created an opening. In 63 BCE he besieged Jerusalem, breached the walls after three months, and entered the Temple — a cultural trauma for the Jewish population, though Pompey restrained his soldiers from looting the Temple treasury, which impressed contemporaries. Judaea became a Roman client state.

Client kingdoms were established or reorganized throughout the region — in Cappadocia, Galatia, Commagene, and along the eastern frontier — creating a buffer zone of nominally independent states that were in practice Roman dependencies.

The financial results were staggering. Plutarch records that Rome's revenues increased by approximately seventy percent as a result of Pompey's eastern settlements. He brought back 480 million sestertii in cash and valuables, paid his soldiers richly, and still had enough left to build Rome's first permanent stone theater — the Theatre of Pompey, which included a new Senate house — funded entirely from the spoils of the East.

## Section 8: The Return and the Political Crisis (62–60 BCE)

### The Man Who Wouldn't March on Rome

Pompey returned to Italy in 62 BCE with an army, enormous wealth, an incomparable reputation, and a set of political problems he proved unable to solve. The core problem was the settlement of his veterans: soldiers who had served in his army expected, upon discharge, to receive land grants — which required senatorial approval. This meant that even Pompey, the most powerful individual in the Roman world, depended on the Senate to ratify his eastern arrangements and provide land for his men.

He had anticipated this. Before returning, he deliberately disbanded his army — an act that stunned observers who widely expected him to march on Rome as Sulla had done. Whether from genuine Republican sentiment, concern about political consequences, or confidence that his prestige would be sufficient, he let his legions go. The decision was a political miscalculation of the first order.

### The Senate Finds Its Spine

Without an army, Pompey was a wealthy private citizen — formidably connected and universally famous, but unable to threaten or coerce. The Senate, led by Marcus Porcius Cato — a rigid defender of what he understood as traditional Republican virtue — chose this moment to reassert its institutional authority.

Pompey made two requests: ratification of his eastern settlement in a single comprehensive bill, and land grants for his veterans. Both were reasonable. Both were blocked. Cato and his allies argued that the eastern settlement needed to be examined clause by clause — a procedurally unanswerable position that guaranteed the process would take years. The land bill was blocked by a combination of senators who objected to specific provisions and others who simply did not want to hand Pompey a political victory. Lucullus returned to Rome and threw himself into the opposition with vindictive energy. Cicero, who had supported Pompey's eastern command and hoped for a close political alliance, found him evasive and unreliable as a partner and gradually distanced himself.

Pompey found himself in the humiliating position of being the greatest military commander in the Roman world and being unable to deliver basic political goods for the men who had served him. He lurched between potential alliances, courting and abandoning partners, and managed to alienate virtually everyone.

### The Stage Is Set

The political crisis created by the Senate's obstruction was the immediate context for the formation of the First Triumvirate — the secret alliance between Pompey, Crassus, and Julius Caesar announced in 60 BCE that would effectively end whatever remained of Republican governance.

Caesar, returning from his praetorship in Spain, offered Pompey what the Senate had refused: a reliable partner who could use the popular assemblies to push through the necessary legislation. In exchange, Caesar wanted the consulship of 59 BCE and a military command in Gaul. Crassus, who had his own grievances — the *publicani* whose tax contracts he represented were seeking a reduction in their obligations that the Senate had also refused — joined the arrangement.

The three men between them controlled more military force, more money, and more political influence than all their opponents combined. The Senate had spent three years demonstrating that institutional obstruction could frustrate even the most powerful individual in Rome. The lesson the three men drew was not that they should accept institutional limits, but that they needed to work together to remove those limits entirely.

Sulla had shown that the Republic could be seized by military force. The triumvirs would show that it could be captured just as effectively through the coordinated manipulation of its own legitimate institutions — elections, legislation, the popular assemblies — by men willing to use them without scruple or restraint.

## Key Terms

- ***Adulescentulus carnifex***: "The young butcher" — the nickname given to Pompey by his critics after his ruthless execution of Marian survivors in Sicily, recorded by Plutarch.
- ***Cursus honorum***: The sequential order of public offices — quaestor, aedile, praetor, consul — that Roman law required citizens to follow. Pompey famously skipped every office below the consulship, rendering his entire early career constitutionally irregular.
- ***Decimation***: The ancient Roman military punishment of executing one soldier in ten (selected by lot) from a unit that had performed dishonorably. Revived by Crassus during the Slave War to restore discipline; it had been essentially unused for over a century.
- ***Ovatio***: A lesser form of the Roman triumph, awarded for victories considered insufficient to merit a full triumph — most commonly for victories over slaves rather than foreign states. Crassus received an ovation for defeating Spartacus.
- ***Lex Gabinia* (67 BCE)**: The law proposed by tribune Aulus Gabinius granting Pompey supreme command over the entire Mediterranean Sea and adjacent coastline to suppress piracy, with authority superior to all other Roman officials in the territory.
- ***Lex Manilia* (66 BCE)**: The law proposed by tribune Gaius Manilius transferring command of the Third Mithridatic War to Pompey, adding authority over all eastern provinces and existing armies to his powers from the *Lex Gabinia*.
- ***Imperium maius***: "Greater authority" — a form of *imperium* superior to that of all other officials in a given area. The powers granted to Pompey by the *Lex Gabinia* effectively constituted an *imperium maius* over the Mediterranean world.
- ***Publicani***: Roman equestrian businessmen who purchased the contracts to collect taxes in Rome's provinces, paying the state a fixed sum in advance and keeping whatever they collected above that amount. Their interests in Asia were damaged by Lucullus's reforms, making them political allies of Pompey and enemies of the Senate.
- ***Clientela***: The Roman system of patronage relationships between powerful patrons and dependent clients. Pompey's eastern settlements created an enormous network of royal and civic clients throughout the eastern Mediterranean, adding to his already vast personal political resources.
- ***Theatre of Pompey***: The first permanent stone theater in Rome, built by Pompey with wealth from his eastern campaigns. It included a new Senate house — the *Curia Pompeia* — in which Julius Caesar would later be assassinated.
- ***Tribuni aerarii***: "Treasury tribunes" — a property class just below the equestrian order. Included as the third element of jury composition under the court reform of 70 BCE, alongside senators and equestrians.
- ***First Triumvirate***: The informal, secret political alliance formed in 60 BCE between Pompey, Crassus, and Caesar. Unlike the later Second Triumvirate, it had no legal basis — it was a private arrangement among three powerful men to coordinate their political resources against the Senate's obstruction.

## Ancient Sources for This Chapter

- **Plutarch** (*Life of Pompey*, *Life of Crassus*, *Life of Sertorius*, *Life of Lucullus*): The essential narrative sources throughout. Plutarch's *Life of Pompey* is detailed and broadly admiring; his *Life of Sertorius* provides an unusually sympathetic portrait of the rebel commander; his *Life of Lucullus* is essential for understanding the transfer of command and includes a corrective perspective on Pompey's eastern reputation.
- **Appian** (*Civil Wars* Book 1, *Mithridatic Wars*): The most systematic surviving account of both the Slave War and the Mithridatic campaigns. Appian's description of Mithridates's failed suicide by poison is one of the most vivid passages in ancient military history. His account of the crucifixion of the six thousand slaves is the primary source for that episode.
- **Cicero** (*Pro lege Manilia / De Imperio Cn. Pompei*): Cicero's speech in favor of the *Lex Manilia* is a primary source of the first importance — not only for the political debate over the command but as a window into how Roman political rhetoric framed questions of individual power and institutional authority at the precise moment when the Republic was visibly failing to govern itself.
- **Sallust** (*Histories*, fragments): Sallust's *Histories* covered this period in detail but survive only in fragments. Pompey's letter to the Senate from Spain — preserved by Sallust — is one of the most important documents of the period, a masterpiece of self-serving political communication in which he simultaneously threatens the Senate and presents himself as its loyal servant.
- **Cassius Dio** (*Roman History*, Books 36–37): Valuable for the political narrative surrounding both the *Lex Gabinia* and *Lex Manilia* and for the senatorial debates over Pompey's commands.
- **Josephus** (*Jewish Antiquities*, *Jewish War*): Essential for Pompey's siege of Jerusalem and the incorporation of Judaea into Rome's eastern settlement, written from a Jewish perspective that provides detail unavailable in the Latin sources.
- **Velleius Paterculus** (*Roman History*): Brief but useful for the political context of Pompey's return and the formation of the First Triumvirate.
- **Strabo** (*Geography*): Strabo traveled extensively through the areas Pompey reorganized and provides invaluable geographical and ethnographic detail about the eastern provinces and client kingdoms of this period.
