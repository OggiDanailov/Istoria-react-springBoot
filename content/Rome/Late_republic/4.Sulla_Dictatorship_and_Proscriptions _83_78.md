
## Section 1: Sulla's Eastern Campaign and the Road Home

While Rome convulsed under the Cinnan regime, Sulla prosecuted his war against Mithridates VI with characteristic efficiency. Between 87 and 85 BCE, his forces recaptured Greece—sacking Athens in March 86 BCE after a prolonged siege—and defeated the Pontic armies at the battles of Chaeronea (86 BCE) and Orchomenus (85 BCE). Both engagements are described in detail by Plutarch in his *Life of Sulla*, who portrays the general as calm under pressure and tactically brilliant.

The Peace of Dardanus, concluded in 85 BCE, ended the First Mithridatic War on terms that many contemporaries considered lenient. Mithridates surrendered his conquests and paid an indemnity of 2,000 talents, but retained his throne and his kingdom of Pontus. Appian records that Sulla's officers questioned why the king who had ordered the massacre of 80,000 Romans was permitted to walk away. The answer was pragmatic: Sulla needed to return to Italy, and a prolonged eastern campaign would have given his enemies in Rome more time to consolidate. Mithridates was a problem for another day.

Sulla spent 84 BCE reorganizing the province of Asia, imposing punishing fines on the cities that had supported Mithridates, and preparing for the confrontation he knew awaited him. His army was now battle-hardened, fiercely loyal, and enriched by eastern plunder. According to Plutarch, Sulla's soldiers called him *Felix*—"the Fortunate"—a title he embraced and later made official.

### The Cinnan Government's Collapse

In Rome, the regime that had ruled since 87 BCE was deteriorating. Cinna, who had held the consulship continuously from 87 to 84 BCE, was killed by his own mutinous soldiers at Ancona in 84 BCE while attempting to organize an army to confront Sulla before he could cross the Adriatic. Appian records the incident as an illustration of the fundamental weakness of the Cinnan government: it lacked a commander who could inspire genuine loyalty from professional soldiers.

The remaining Marian leaders—Gnaeus Papirius Carbo and the younger Gaius Marius (son of the famous general)—attempted to prepare for Sulla's return, but their position was undermined by political divisions, uncertain military loyalty, and the knowledge that Sulla's veterans were far more experienced than their own forces. Velleius Paterculus notes that many Italian communities, despite having received citizenship from the Cinnan government, were already calculating which side was more likely to prevail.

## Section 2: The Civil War in Italy (83–82 BCE)

### Sulla's Landing and Early Campaign

In the spring of 83 BCE, Sulla crossed the Adriatic with approximately 40,000 veterans and landed at Brundisium (modern Brindisi) in southern Italy. He immediately issued a public declaration, recorded by Appian, that he bore no hostility toward Rome or its citizens—only toward those who had wronged him and usurped power illegally. It was a calculated political gesture: Sulla presented himself not as an invader but as a defender of constitutional order.

The declaration had its intended effect. Defections from the Marian cause began almost immediately. Most significant among these was the young Gnaeus Pompeius—later known as Pompey the Great—who raised three legions from his family's clients in Picenum and marched south to join Sulla. He was twenty-three years old. Plutarch records that Sulla greeted him with the honorific title *imperator*, an extraordinary gesture toward someone who held no magistracy and had no legal authority to command troops. It was an early sign that Sulla, for all his professed devotion to constitutional norms, was willing to bend those norms when it suited his purposes.

Other prominent Romans joined Sulla's march: Marcus Licinius Crassus, who had been hiding in Spain since his father's death during the Marian massacres, arrived with a small force; Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius, son of Marius's old rival Metellus Numidicus, brought additional troops from Africa. The coalition assembling around Sulla represented a broad cross-section of the Roman aristocracy—men driven to his side not necessarily by shared ideology, but by the calculation that his victory was probable and that being on the losing side would mean death.

### The Campaign of 83–82 BCE

The civil war that followed was fought across the Italian peninsula in a series of engagements that Appian and Plutarch describe in considerable detail. The Marian forces were numerically superior—various estimates suggest they fielded between 100,000 and 200,000 men across multiple armies—but they suffered from divided command, inconsistent troop quality, and the defection of key allies.

Sulla advanced methodically northward through 83 BCE, winning engagements at Mount Tifata near Capua and forcing several Marian commanders to withdraw. The younger Marius, who had been elected consul for 82 BCE at the age of twenty-six, attempted to halt Sulla's advance at Sacriportus (near modern Palestrina) in early 82 BCE. The battle was decisive: Marius's army broke, and he was forced to retreat to the fortified city of Praeneste, where he was besieged for the remainder of the war.

The Samnites, Rome's old enemies from the Social War, entered the conflict on the Marian side—not from loyalty to the Marian cause, but from the recognition that a Sullan victory would bring retribution for their earlier rebellion. Their intervention added a dangerous new dimension to the conflict and nearly altered its outcome.

### The Battle of the Colline Gate (1 November 82 BCE)

The climactic engagement of the civil war occurred at the Colline Gate, on the northeastern edge of Rome itself. A combined force of Samnites and Marian loyalists, commanded by Pontius Telesinus, made a desperate march on the capital while Sulla's forces were engaged elsewhere. Telesinus reportedly declared that the "wolves" preying on Italian liberty would never be destroyed until the forest that sheltered them—Rome itself—was cut down. Velleius Paterculus preserves this statement as evidence of how deeply the hatreds of the Social War still burned.

Sulla arrived with his army and engaged the enemy force outside the walls on the afternoon of November 1, 82 BCE. The battle was chaotic and extended into the night. Plutarch records that Sulla's left wing was driven back and nearly routed, while his right wing achieved a breakthrough. The fighting was among the bloodiest of the entire civil war period. Appian estimates that approximately 50,000 men died on both sides, though such figures in ancient sources are frequently inflated.

By morning, the Marian-Samnite force was destroyed. Telesinus was found among the dead. Sulla had won the civil war. What remained was to determine the nature of the peace.

## Section 3: *Dictator Legibus Scribundis et Rei Publicae Constituendae*

### An Ancient Office Revived

In late 82 BCE, following his victory at the Colline Gate, Sulla arranged for his appointment to an office that had not been used in over a century: the dictatorship. The last regular dictator had been appointed in 202 BCE, during the Second Punic War. The office was a constitutional mechanism for emergency rule—a single individual granted supreme authority for a maximum of six months to resolve a specific crisis.

Sulla's version was fundamentally different. The Senate passed a law—the *Lex Valeria*—appointing him *dictator legibus scribundis et rei publicae constituendae*: "dictator for the writing of laws and the reconstitution of the state." There was no six-month limit. There was no specific crisis to resolve and then step down. The appointment was, as Appian observes, unprecedented in Roman history: an indefinite, legally unlimited autocracy.

The mechanism by which this occurred reveals the hollowness of Republican forms by this point. With both consuls dead—the younger Marius had committed suicide at Praeneste, and Carbo had been captured and executed in Sicily by Pompey—the Senate appointed an *interrex* (a temporary placeholder magistrate), Lucius Valerius Flaccus, who proposed the law granting Sulla dictatorial power. The Senate ratified it. The popular assemblies confirmed it. Every constitutional formality was observed. The result was absolute monarchy in Republican dress.

Sulla now possessed the power of life and death over every citizen, the authority to make and repeal laws without appeal, the right to confiscate property, found colonies, and reorganize the entire structure of the state. Appian notes that he was also granted retroactive immunity for all his past actions, including the march on Rome in 88 BCE.

## Section 4: The Proscriptions

### Innovation in Political Murder

What followed Sulla's appointment was, in the assessment of virtually every ancient source, the most horrifying episode in Roman political history to that date. The Marian massacres of 87 BCE had been chaotic and personal—Marius pointing at victims, his slave-soldiers carrying out killings on the streets. Sulla's approach was bureaucratic.

The **proscriptions** (*proscriptiones*) were published lists of Roman citizens declared enemies of the state. Anyone whose name appeared on the lists was stripped of all legal protections: they could be killed by anyone, their property was confiscated by the state, and their descendants were permanently barred from holding public office. A bounty was offered for each head—according to Plutarch, two talents (a substantial sum) per victim—and anyone who sheltered a proscribed person faced execution themselves.

The first list appeared in November 82 BCE and contained eighty names. A second list followed the next day with an additional 220. More lists came in the following days and weeks. Appian records that a total of approximately 4,700 Roman citizens were proscribed, including 40 senators and 1,600 members of the equestrian order (*equites*)—the wealthy business class that controlled much of Rome's commercial economy. Plutarch gives slightly different numbers but confirms the scale.

### The Mechanics of Terror

The proscriptions operated through a calculated system designed to maximize both terror and profit. Sulla posted the lists publicly in the Forum, where any citizen could read them. Plutarch describes the atmosphere: men would approach the lists with dread, scanning for their own names or the names of friends and family. Finding a name meant an immediate death sentence—not by the state's official executioners, but by any citizen or soldier willing to collect the bounty.

The bodies of the proscribed were denied proper burial, a punishment that struck at deep Roman religious sensibilities. Their property was auctioned publicly, often at a fraction of its value, to Sulla's supporters and allies. Plutarch records that Sulla himself presided over some of these auctions, selling the estates of murdered men while making contemptuous remarks about the bargains available. The historian notes that senators who had previously considered themselves neutral were horrified to discover that the proscriptions were not merely political retribution but an economic redistribution program designed to reward Sulla's faction.

Marcus Licinius Crassus, who had joined Sulla's cause early, used the proscriptions to build an enormous personal fortune by purchasing confiscated properties at artificially depressed prices. Plutarch, in his *Life of Crassus*, notes that this aggressive profiteering eventually embarrassed even Sulla, who distanced himself from Crassus despite their political alliance.

### The Scope of Retribution

The proscriptions targeted several categories of victims. The first and most obvious were active participants in the Marian-Cinnan regime: magistrates, military commanders, and their prominent supporters. The second category comprised personal enemies of Sulla or his allies—men who had given offence at some point during the previous decade of political upheaval. The third, and most troubling to ancient and modern historians alike, were wealthy individuals who appear to have been proscribed primarily for their property.

Appian records an incident that became emblematic of the proscriptions' arbitrary cruelty: a man named Quintus Aurelius, who had no involvement in politics whatsoever, saw his name on the list while walking through the Forum. "Alas," he reportedly said, "my Alban estate is pursuing me"—meaning that his valuable property, not any political act, had sealed his fate. He was killed shortly afterward.

The inclusion of the equestrian order in such large numbers supports this interpretation. These men were, by definition, wealthy but largely apolitical—businessmen, tax collectors, and landowners rather than senators and magistrates. Their proscription served the dual purpose of eliminating potential financial support for future opposition and generating revenue to pay Sulla's veterans.

### A Young Caesar

One name that nearly appeared on the proscription lists was that of Gaius Julius Caesar, then eighteen years old. Caesar was connected to the Marian faction through multiple ties: his aunt Julia had been Marius's wife, and Caesar himself had married Cornelia, the daughter of Cinna. Sulla reportedly demanded that Caesar divorce Cornelia as a condition of his safety. According to Suetonius, who drew on earlier sources, Caesar refused—an act of remarkable courage or remarkable recklessness for a teenager facing a dictator who had killed thousands.

Caesar went into hiding, and influential friends eventually persuaded Sulla to remove his name from consideration. Plutarch and Suetonius both record Sulla's reported response: that those who interceded did not understand what they were protecting, for in this young man he saw "many a Marius." Whether the anecdote is authentic or retrospective invention, it captures the reality that the proscriptions, for all their thoroughness, could not eliminate every future threat.

## Section 5: Constitutional Reforms—Restoring the Republic by Rewriting It

### Sulla's Political Vision

Having secured absolute power and eliminated his enemies, Sulla turned to the stated purpose of his dictatorship: the reconstitution of the Roman state. His reforms, enacted primarily in 81 BCE through a comprehensive legislative program, represented the most thorough restructuring of the Roman constitution since the Conflict of the Orders in the early Republic.

Understanding Sulla's reforms requires understanding his political position. He was, in the terminology of Roman politics, an *optimas*—a defender of senatorial authority against the populist methods (*populares*) that men like the Gracchi, Saturninus, and Marius had employed. His constitutional program was designed to restore the Senate's supremacy and to prevent any future individual from accumulating the kind of power that Marius, Cinna, and—paradoxically—Sulla himself had wielded.

The irony was not lost on contemporaries. As Appian observes, Sulla used autocratic power to legislate against autocracy, and violated every constitutional norm in order to enshrine those norms in law.

### The Reforms

**The Tribunate Neutralized**

Sulla's most consequential reform targeted the office of tribune of the plebs (*tribunus plebis*). The tribunate had been the primary vehicle through which populist politicians had challenged senatorial authority—the Gracchi, Saturninus, Sulpicius, and Cinna had all used the office to bypass the Senate and legislate directly through the popular assemblies. Sulla effectively gutted the office. Tribunes were prohibited from proposing legislation to the assemblies without prior senatorial approval. Their power of veto (*intercessio*) was curtailed. Most critically, anyone who held the tribunate was permanently barred from holding any further magistracy. This last provision ensured that no ambitious politician would seek the office, since accepting it would end their career.

**The *Cursus Honorum* Codified**

The *cursus honorum*—the sequence of magistracies that constituted a Roman political career—was given strict legal definition. Minimum ages were established for each office: thirty for the quaestorship, thirty-nine for the praetorship, and forty-two for the consulship. A mandatory interval of two years was required between holding successive offices. No magistracy could be held a second time until ten years had elapsed. These regulations were designed to prevent the kind of repeated consulships that Marius and Cinna had used to maintain power.

**The Senate Expanded**

Sulla increased the Senate's membership from approximately 300 to 600, enrolling large numbers of equestrians and his own supporters. This served multiple purposes: it diluted the influence of any single faction, it rewarded loyalty, and it created a larger pool of administrators for Rome's growing provincial responsibilities.

**The Courts Reorganized**

The standing criminal courts (*quaestiones perpetuae*) were expanded and systematized. Crucially, Sulla transferred jury service in these courts from the equestrian order back to the Senate—reversing the reform that Gaius Gracchus had enacted decades earlier. This change restored senatorial control over the judiciary and eliminated a significant source of equestrian political influence.

**Provincial Commands Limited**

Governors were prohibited from leaving their provinces, making war, or crossing provincial boundaries without explicit authorization from the Senate. Commanders were required to disband their armies within a specified period after their term ended. These measures were transparently aimed at preventing a repetition of what Sulla himself had done—using a provincial army to march on Rome.

**The Priesthoods**

Sulla returned the selection of priests and augurs to the traditional method of co-optation by existing members of the priestly colleges, reversing the popular election that had been introduced by the *Lex Domitia* in 104 BCE. This seemingly minor change reinforced aristocratic control over Roman religion, which was deeply intertwined with political authority.

### The Contradictions

The fundamental problem with Sulla's constitutional program was not its internal logic—which was coherent—but the circumstances of its enactment. Every reform was imposed by a dictator wielding unlimited power obtained through military force. The Senate whose authority he championed had been purged of his opponents and packed with his supporters. The courts he reorganized were staffed by men who owed their positions to him. The constitution he restored was one that had demonstrably failed to prevent the crises of the previous decades.

Cicero, writing years later, offered a characteristically ambivalent assessment. He acknowledged the intellectual coherence of Sulla's reforms while noting that they were enacted through the very methods they sought to prevent. The constitution could not be restored by unconstitutional means and retain its moral authority.

## Section 6: Retirement and Death

### The Abdication

In 79 BCE, Sulla did something that no one—neither his allies nor his enemies—had expected. He resigned the dictatorship and retired to private life.

The ancient sources treat this decision with a mixture of astonishment and suspicion. Plutarch devotes considerable attention to the scene: Sulla appeared in the Forum, formally laid down his authority, dismissed his lictors (the attendants who symbolized a magistrate's power), and announced that he was available to answer questions from any citizen about his conduct. According to Plutarch, the crowd was too stunned—or too terrified—to respond. Sulla then walked home through the streets of Rome accompanied by only a few friends, and no one touched him.

Appian interprets the resignation as evidence of Sulla's genuine belief that his reforms had resolved the Republic's structural problems. Having rewritten the constitution, he considered his work complete. Velleius Paterculus, writing under the Emperor Tiberius, presents it as an act of extraordinary self-control rare in human history.

Modern scholars have offered less generous interpretations. Sulla may have calculated that permanent dictatorship would eventually provoke the kind of opposition that would undo his reforms, while voluntary retirement would lend them legitimacy. He may also have been in declining health—Plutarch describes symptoms consistent with advanced liver disease and a chronic skin condition—and preferred to die as a private citizen rather than in office.

Whatever his reasoning, the resignation was genuine. Sulla retired to his estate in Campania, where Plutarch records that he spent his final months writing his memoirs (now lost), entertaining actors and theatrical performers, and drinking heavily. He held one final consulship in 80 BCE before his retirement but exercised the office conventionally.

### Death and Funeral

Lucius Cornelius Sulla Felix died in 78 BCE at approximately sixty years of age. Plutarch describes his death in clinical detail: he suffered from a condition that caused his flesh to ulcerate and breed lice—a description consistent with several possible diagnoses, though ancient medical terminology makes certainty impossible. Appian adds that he suffered a hemorrhage after a bout of anger directed at a local magistrate who had failed to repay a debt to the public treasury.

His funeral was the grandest Rome had seen. Despite the fears of the consul Lepidus, who opposed granting state honors to the dead dictator, Sulla's veterans converged on Rome in such numbers that opposition was futile. His body was carried through the streets in a magnificent procession, accompanied by his former soldiers, and cremated on the Campus Martius. Plutarch records that the women of Rome contributed such quantities of spices and incense that, in addition to filling 210 litters, an image of Sulla himself was molded from frankincense and cinnamon.

His self-composed epitaph, preserved by Plutarch, declared that no friend had ever surpassed him in kindness, and no enemy in harm. It was, by any measure, an accurate assessment.

## Section 7: The Legacy of Sulla

### The Immediate Aftermath

Sulla's constitutional settlement began to unravel almost immediately after his death. In 78 BCE, the consul Marcus Aemilius Lepidus attempted to overturn the Sullan reforms and was suppressed only by military force—commanded by Pompey, who was once again acting without legal authority. By 75 BCE, the restrictions on the tribunate were being loosened. By 70 BCE, Pompey and Crassus—both products of the Sullan system—restored the tribunate's full powers during their joint consulship, dismantling the centerpiece of Sulla's program.

The colonies of veterans that Sulla had established throughout Italy on confiscated land became sources of resentment and instability. Dispossessed farmers and their descendants harbored grievances that would fuel future unrest, including the conspiracy of Catiline in 63 BCE—many of whose supporters were men ruined by Sullan confiscations.

### The Deeper Legacy

Sulla's most enduring legacy was not his constitution, which barely survived a decade, but the precedent he established. He had demonstrated, beyond any possibility of denial, that the Roman Republic could be seized by military force, that political opponents could be systematically eliminated through legal mechanisms, and that a single individual could reshape the entire state according to his will.

The proscriptions established a template that would be repeated. When the Second Triumvirate of Octavian, Antony, and Lepidus seized power in 43 BCE, they implemented their own proscriptions directly modeled on Sulla's. The most prominent victim was Cicero himself, who had survived Sulla's lists as a young man only to die on the lists of Sulla's political heirs.

The dictatorship demonstrated that autocratic power, once wielded, could not be neutralized simply by being voluntarily surrendered. Sulla's retirement did not restore genuine Republican government; it merely created a period in which competing strongmen—Pompey, Crassus, and eventually Caesar—maneuvered for the kind of supremacy Sulla had shown was possible.

Sallust, writing a generation later, identified Sulla's dictatorship as the moment when the moral foundations of the Republic collapsed irreparably. The historian argued that the proscriptions and confiscations had taught Romans that power and wealth acquired through violence could be enjoyed with impunity, and that this lesson corrupted public life at every level.

Perhaps most significantly, Sulla had proven that the Roman constitution possessed no mechanism for preventing a determined military commander from seizing power. The *pomerium*, the consular authority, the tribunician veto, the Senate's *auctoritas*—all the institutional safeguards that were supposed to prevent tyranny had failed completely when confronted with professional legions loyal to their general rather than to the state. Every subsequent crisis of the Republic would be shaped by this knowledge.

Julius Caesar, crossing the Rubicon in 49 BCE, was following a path that Sulla had cleared. The difference was that Caesar, unlike Sulla, would not retire.

## Key Terms

- ***Proscriptiones* (Proscriptions)**: Published lists of citizens declared enemies of the state, subject to execution, property confiscation, and the political exclusion of their descendants. First used systematically by Sulla in 82 BCE.
- ***Dictator legibus scribundis et rei publicae constituendae***: "Dictator for the writing of laws and the reconstitution of the state"—the unprecedented title granted to Sulla, with no time limit, distinguishing it from the traditional six-month emergency dictatorship.
- ***Lex Valeria***: The law proposed by the *interrex* Lucius Valerius Flaccus granting Sulla dictatorial power in late 82 BCE.
- ***Cursus honorum***: The sequential order of public offices that constituted a Roman political career, formalized by Sulla with minimum ages and mandatory intervals between offices.
- ***Intercessio***: The tribune's power of veto, which could block any act of a magistrate or any piece of legislation. Severely curtailed under Sulla's reforms.
- ***Quaestiones perpetuae***: Permanent standing courts for criminal trials, reorganized by Sulla with senatorial rather than equestrian juries.
- ***Felix***: "The Fortunate"—the surname Sulla adopted, claiming divine favor for his victories and political success.
- ***Interrex***: A temporary magistrate appointed to oversee elections when both consuls were dead or absent. Lucius Valerius Flaccus served in this role to facilitate Sulla's appointment.
- ***Lictors***: Attendants who accompanied Roman magistrates carrying the *fasces* (bundles of rods), symbolizing the magistrate's authority to punish. Sulla's dismissal of his lictors upon resignation symbolized his return to private status.
- ***Campus Martius***: The "Field of Mars" outside the *pomerium*, used for military assemblies, elections, and, in the case of distinguished Romans, cremation.

## Ancient Sources for This Chapter

- **Plutarch** (*Life of Sulla*, *Life of Crassus*, *Life of Pompey*, *Life of Caesar*): Greek biographer writing c. 100 CE. Provides the most detailed narrative of Sulla's dictatorship, proscriptions, and retirement. Rich in anecdote and personal detail, though writing over 150 years after the events.
- **Appian** (*Civil Wars*, Book 1): Greek historian writing c. 150 CE. Offers the most systematic account of the proscriptions, including estimated numbers. Particularly valuable for the political and military narrative.
- **Velleius Paterculus** (*Roman History*): Roman historian writing c. 30 CE under Emperor Tiberius. Briefer than Plutarch or Appian, but closer in time to the events. Notably sympathetic to Sulla.
- **Suetonius** (*Life of the Deified Julius Caesar*): Roman biographer writing c. 120 CE. Source for the anecdote of young Caesar's defiance of Sulla.
- **Sallust** (*Bellum Catilinae*, *Histories*): Roman historian writing c. 40 BCE—the closest in time to Sulla's era. Provides important analysis of Sulla's impact on Roman morality and political culture. The *Histories*, which covered this period in detail, survive only in fragments.
- **Cicero** (various speeches and letters): Contemporary of Sulla who lived through the proscriptions as a young man. His observations are scattered across his works but are invaluable as firsthand testimony.
- **Livy** (*Periochae*): The relevant books of Livy's monumental *Ab Urbe Condita* are lost; only brief summaries (*Periochae*) survive for this period.
